## 1.1 Commercial Invoice

**Legal Authority:** 19 CFR 141.86 (Contents of invoices and general requirements); 19 CFR 141.83 (Type of invoice required); 19 CFR 142.6 (Invoice requirements)

The commercial invoice is the foundational document for every customs entry. Per 19 CFR 141.86(a), each invoice of imported merchandise must set forth the following information:

**Required Fields (19 CFR 141.86(a)):**
1. **Port of entry** to which the merchandise is destined
2. **Sale/shipment details** -- the time when, the place where, and the person by whom and to whom the merchandise is sold or agreed to be sold; or if not pursuant to a purchase, the place from which shipped, the time, and the parties
3. **Detailed description** -- the name by which each item is known, grade or quality, marks, numbers, and symbols under which sold by the seller/manufacturer in the country of exportation, plus marks and numbers of packages
4. **Quantities** -- in the weights and measures of the country of export or in U.S. weights and measures
5. **Purchase price** -- of each item in the currency of the purchase
6. **Value** -- if not purchased, the value in currency of the country of exportation
7. **Kind of currency** used in the transaction
8. **All charges upon the merchandise** -- itemized by name and amount, including freight, insurance, commission, cases, containers, coverings, and cost of packing
9. **All rebates, drawbacks, and bounties** -- separately itemized, allowed upon exportation
10. **Country of origin** of the merchandise
11. **All goods or services furnished for production** (assists) -- dies, molds, tools, engineering work not included in invoice price. Goods/services furnished in the U.S. are excluded. Annual reports accepted when approved by the Center director.

**Additional Requirements:**
- **19 CFR 141.86(d):** Invoice and all attachments must be in English, or have an accurate English translation
- **19 CFR 141.86(e):** Must state in adequate detail what merchandise is in each individual package (packing list function)
- **19 CFR 141.86(g):** Must set forth every discount from list or base price for each class of merchandise
- **19 CFR 141.86(j):** Must identify by name a responsible employee of the exporter who has knowledge of the transaction
- **19 CFR 141.90:** The importer or broker must include the appropriate full 10-digit HTS subheading and rate of duty

**Critical fields for customs valuation purposes:**
- Seller name and address
- Buyer name and address
- Description of goods sufficient for HS classification
- Harmonized System (HS) code
- Country of origin
- Transaction value in specified currency
- Incoterm and named place (determines what costs are included in declared price)
- Currency of transaction
- All assists, royalties, and proceeds

### 1.2 Packing List

The packing list is required under 19 CFR 141.86(e) and supplements the commercial invoice. It must state in adequate detail:

- **Contents of each individual package** -- itemized merchandise per carton/pallet/container
- **Gross weight and net weight** per package (in kilograms and/or pounds)
- **Dimensions** of each package (length x width x height)
- **Package marks and numbers** -- corresponding to marks on the commercial invoice
- **Total number of packages** in the shipment
- **Total gross and net weight** of the entire shipment
- **Packaging type** (cartons, pallets, crates, drums, etc.)

Per 19 CFR 141.86(f), if the invoice or entry does not disclose the weight, gage, or measure necessary to ascertain duties, the consignee must pay the expense of weighing, gaging, or measuring prior to release from CBP custody.

### 1.3 Bill of Lading (B/L)

A bill of lading is a document issued by a carrier (or its agent) acknowledging receipt of cargo for shipment. It serves three critical legal functions: (1) receipt for shipped goods, (2) document of title / evidence of ownership, and (3) contract of carriage between shipper and carrier.

**Ocean B/L Types:**

| Type | Description | Negotiable? |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Straight B/L** | Non-negotiable; consigned to a specific named party who must receive delivery. Cannot be endorsed or transferred. Used when goods are prepaid or are gifts/donations. | No |
| **Order B/L** | Negotiable instrument; gives the holder rights over cargo. Can be endorsed and transferred. Used for shipments prior to payment; banks typically require order B/Ls under letters of credit. | Yes |
| **Clean B/L** | States cargo loaded in apparent good order and condition. No clauses noting defects. Required by banks under L/C transactions. | N/A (attribute) |
| **Dirty (Claused) B/L** | Notes defects in goods or packaging condition. Also called "foul" or "soiled" B/L. Banks entitled to reject. | N/A (attribute) |

**Master B/L vs. House B/L:**

| Feature | Master Bill of Lading (MBL) | House Bill of Lading (HBL) |
|---------|----------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Issued by** | Ocean carrier (shipping line) | Freight forwarder or NVOCC |
| **Parties** | Carrier to forwarder/NVOCC | Forwarder to actual shipper/consignee |
| **Contract** | Direct carrier contract of carriage | Forwarder's contract; not the carrier's contract |
| **Use case** | Direct shipper-carrier relationship; FCL | Consolidated shipments; LCL |
| **CBP filing** | Carrier files manifest data from MBL | Forwarder may file supplemental data |

### 1.4 Air Waybill (AWB)

**Legal Framework:** Warsaw Convention (1929), Hague Protocol, Montreal Convention (1999); IATA Resolution 600b (Conditions of Contract); IATA Resolution 672 (Electronic AWB)

An air waybill is a transport document that accompanies goods shipped by air carrier. It is governed by Article 15 of the Warsaw Convention (as amended).

**Critical characteristic: AWBs are inherently NON-NEGOTIABLE.** The words "non-negotiable" are printed at the top. An AWB is a receipt for goods and a contract for transportation only -- it does NOT transfer ownership of merchandise. This contrasts with ocean bills of lading, which can be negotiable documents of title.

**Master AWB (MAWB) vs. House AWB (HAWB):**

| Feature | MAWB | HAWB |
|---------|------|------|
| **Issued by** | Air carrier or its authorized agent | Freight forwarder |
| **Subject to IATA rules** | Yes (mandatory) | May or may not be |
| **Subject to Warsaw/Montreal Convention** | Yes | Not necessarily |
| **Contains carrier's contract** | Yes | No -- shipper on HAWB is not a direct party to carrier's carriage contract |
| **Legal protection for loss/damage** | Full protection under conventions | Limited |
| **AWB number format** | 11 digits (3-digit airline prefix + 8-digit serial) | Forwarder's own numbering |

AWBs are issued in eight sets of different colors: green (carrier's copy), pink (consignee's copy), blue (shipper's copy), plus five additional copies for various purposes.

Since January 1, 2019, the electronic AWB (e-AWB) is the default contract of carriage per IATA Resolution 672. Paper AWBs remain acceptable but IATA members have largely transitioned to electronic.

### 1.5 Certificate of Origin

**When Required:**
- Claiming preferential duty rates under a Free Trade Agreement (FTA)
- Country of origin marking verification (19 CFR Part 134)
- Anti-dumping/countervailing duty cases where origin is material
- Quota administration
- Government procurement

**Preferential vs. Non-Preferential:**

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Preferential** | Claims reduced or zero duty rates under an FTA. Must meet specific rules of origin (tariff shift, regional value content, etc.) | USMCA certification, CAFTA-DR certificate |
| **Non-Preferential** | Establishes country of manufacture for MFN duty rates, marking, trade statistics. Does NOT provide duty reduction. | Generic C/O issued by chamber of commerce |

**USMCA Certificate of Origin (replacing NAFTA Form 434):**

The USMCA does not prescribe a specific form. Instead, it requires **nine minimum data elements** per Annex 5-A, which can appear on an invoice or any other document:

1. Indication that it is a certification of origin (importer, exporter, or producer)
2. Certifier (name, title, address, telephone, email)
3. Exporter (name, address, if different from certifier)
4. Producer (name, address, if different from certifier/exporter)
5. Importer (name, address)
6. Description and HS tariff classification of the good (6-digit level minimum)
7. Origin criterion (A, B, C, or D)
8. Blanket period (if applicable -- up to 12 months)
9. Authorized signature and date

**Origin Criteria:**
- **A:** Wholly obtained or produced entirely in one or more USMCA countries
- **B:** Produced entirely in USMCA territory using non-originating materials that satisfy product-specific rules of origin (Annex 4-B)
- **C:** Produced entirely from originating materials
- **D:** Produced in USMCA territory, does not satisfy applicable rule of origin but meets Regional Value Content (RVC) requirements

**Exemptions:** No certification required for non-commercial importations or commercial importations where originating goods value does not exceed US $2,500 (unless part of a series designed to evade).

The certifier can be the importer, exporter, or producer. The CBP Form 434 (NAFTA) is no longer accepted. Records must be maintained to substantiate originating status.

**Legal Authority:** USMCA Chapter 5, Annex 5-A; 19 CFR Part 182

### 1.6 Entry Summary (CBP Form 7501)

**Legal Authority:** 19 CFR Part 141; 19 CFR Part 142; 19 U.S.C. 1484

CBP Form 7501 is the definitive document for determining classification, valuation, origin, and duty assessment for imported merchandise. It must be filed within **10 working days** of the release of merchandise.

**Key Fields/Blocks:**

| Block | Field | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Entry Number | 11-digit format: XXX-NNNNNNN-N (filer code + unique number + check digit) |
| 2 | Entry Type | Two-digit code: first digit = general category (0=consumption, 1=informal, 2=warehouse), second = processing type |
| 3 | Summary Date | Date entry summary is filed |
| 4 | Surety Number | Three-digit code identifying the surety company on the customs bond |
| 5 | Bond Type | Single transaction vs. continuous bond |
| 7 | Entry Date | Date of entry (when goods released) |
| 10 | Country of Origin | Country of manufacture, production, or growth. Where multiple countries involved, where last substantial transformation occurred |
| 14 | Importing Carrier | Name or code of carrier that transported goods |
| 15 | Country of Export | Country from which goods were last exported |
| 19 | Mode of Transport | Code for transport mode (10=vessel, 40=air, 11=vessel/container) |
| 23 | Importer Number | IRS number, SSN, or CBP-assigned number |
| 27 | Line data | Individual commodity lines |
| 28 | HTS number | Full 10-digit HTS classification code (column 28A) |
| 29 | Description | Merchandise description |
| 30 | Gross Weight | In kilograms |
| 31 | Net Quantity | In HTS unit of quantity |
| 32 | Entered Value | Transaction value in U.S. dollars |
| 33 | Charges | Non-dutiable charges (freight, insurance if CIF terms) |
| 34 | Relationship | Whether buyer and seller are related (Y/N) |
| 35 | Rate of Duty | Ad valorem percentage or specific rate from HTS |
| 36 | Duty Amount | Calculated: Entered Value x Rate of Duty |
| 37-39 | Taxes and Fees | IR tax, other fees, total duties/taxes/fees |

**Duty Calculation on Form 7501:**
- Entered Value (Block 32) is the appraised transaction value in USD
- Duty = Entered Value x applicable duty rate from HTS
- Plus: Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF) -- 0.3464% of entered value (min $31.67, max $614.35 for FY2025 formal entries)
- Plus: Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF) -- 0.125% of value for ocean shipments
- All values reported in USD using the exchange rate in effect on the date of exportation

### 1.7 Entry/Immediate Delivery (CBP Form 3461)

**Legal Authority:** 19 U.S.C. 1484 (entry), 19 U.S.C. 1448(b) (immediate delivery); 19 CFR Part 142

**Purpose:** CBP Form 3461 requests the release of goods from CBP custody. It is the initial filing that triggers cargo examination and release, before the full Entry Summary (Form 7501) is filed.

**Two Procedures:**
1. **Entry** (19 U.S.C. 1484) -- Standard entry procedure
2. **Immediate Delivery** (19 U.S.C. 1448(b)) -- Expedited release for pre-approved shipments; Form 3461 filed prior to arrival

**Timing:**
- Within **15 calendar days** of arrival at a U.S. port of entry, entry documents must be filed (CBP Form 7533 Entry Manifest or CBP Form 3461)
- For **immediate delivery**: Form 3461 may be filed **before** vessel/aircraft arrival; if approved, goods released expeditiously upon arrival
- After release: **Entry Summary (Form 7501) must be filed within 10 working days** of release, along with estimated duty deposit

**Key Information on Form 3461:**
- Entry number and entry type
- Shipment description, HTS codes, quantities, packaging
- Consignee, manufacturer, seller, buyer
- Port of entry
- Bond information

CBP Form 3461 is also a **delivery authorization document** -- it is presented to the importing carrier to authorize physical release of merchandise.

### 1.8 Importer Security Filing (ISF / 10+2)

**Legal Authority:** Security and Accountability for Every (SAFE) Port Act of 2006; 19 CFR Part 149; CBP Final Rule effective January 26, 2009

**Applicability:** All **ocean** containerized cargo destined for the United States. Bulk cargo is exempt. Does not apply to air freight.

**The "10" Data Elements from the Importer** (filed no later than 24 hours before cargo is laden aboard the vessel):

| # | Data Element | Details |
|---|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Seller (or owner) | Name and address |
| 2 | Buyer (or owner) | Name and address |
| 3 | Importer of record number | IRS/EIN or CBP-assigned number; or FTZ applicant ID |
| 4 | Consignee number | IRS/EIN or CBP-assigned number |
| 5 | Manufacturer (or supplier) | Name and address |
| 6 | Ship-to party | Name and address |
| 7 | Country of origin | ISO country code |
| 8 | Commodity HTSUS number | 6-digit level minimum |
| 9 | Container stuffing location | Name and address of facility where goods were stuffed into container |
| 10 | Consolidator (stuffer) | Name and address of party who stuffed the container |

**Flexible elements:** Manufacturer, ship-to party, country of origin, and HTS may be filed as "best known" data ("Flexible Range") and updated as better information becomes available, no later than 24 hours before vessel arrival at U.S. port.

**The "+2" Data Elements from the Carrier:**

| # | Data Element | Timing |
|---|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Vessel Stow Plan | No later than 48 hours after departure from last foreign port (or before arrival if voyage < 48 hours) |
| 2 | Container Status Messages (CSM) | No later than 24 hours after message entered into carrier's equipment tracking system; includes event code, container number, date/time, empty/full status, location, vessel ID |

**ISF-5 (Reduced Filing):** For IE (Immediate Exportation), T&E (Transportation and Exportation), and FROB cargo -- only 5 data elements required.

**Penalties:** Failure to file accurately and on time can result in **$5,000 per violation**. Goods may be held ("ISF jail") pending compliant filing. Full enforcement since June 30, 2016.

**Filing Method:** Electronically via Automated Broker Interface (ABI) or Automated Manifest System (AMS). An importer's existing continuous bond covers ISF obligations.

### 1.9 Vessel Arrival and Manifest Processing

**Legal Authority:** 19 CFR Part 4 (Vessels in Foreign and Domestic Trades); 19 U.S.C. 1431

**Manifest Requirements (19 CFR 4.7):**
- The master of every vessel arriving and required to make entry must have on board a complete manifest
- Manifest consists of Vessel Entrance or Clearance Statement (CBP Form 1300) and associated documents
- Cargo Declaration (CBP Form 1302) must list all inward foreign cargo regardless of U.S. port of discharge, plus any Foreign Remaining On Board (FROB) cargo separately

**24-Hour Advance Cargo Declaration Rule:**
- Vessel agents must submit electronic Cargo Declaration (CBP Form 1302 equivalent) **24 hours before cargo is laden aboard the vessel** at the foreign port
- Bulk cargo carriers: electronic declaration due **24 hours prior to vessel arrival** in the U.S. (not 24 hours before lading)
- Break bulk cargo: similar timing to bulk

**Preliminary Entry (19 CFR 4.9):**
- Allows vessel to commence lading/unlading operations prior to formal entry
- May be accomplished electronically
- Conditioned upon presentation of complete manifest (Cargo Declaration must be electronic)
- Grants preliminary vessel entry, enabling faster cargo release

**Formal Entry Requirements:**
- Vessel agent must submit CBP Form 1300, Cargo Declaration, crew/passenger lists
- Certificates required: Certificate of Financial Responsibility, Load Line Certificate, term permit
- If vessel arrives in ballast with no cargo, "No merchandise on board" noted on Form 1300

**Penalties (19 U.S.C. 1584):** Unmanifested articles subject to forfeiture; master liable for penalty equal to value of unmanifested goods. Forged, altered, or false documents also subject to penalty.

### 1.10 Power of Attorney (POA)

**Legal Authority:** 19 CFR Part 141, Subpart C (141.31-141.46); CBP Form 5291

**Purpose:** Authorizes a licensed customs broker to act as the importer's agent to prepare and file customs documents, make entries, pay duties, and transact all customs business on behalf of the principal.

**Key Requirements:**
- **19 CFR 141.46:** Before transacting customs business, a broker must obtain a valid POA. The broker is **not** required to file it with CBP but must retain it in records and produce it on demand.
- **Form:** CBP Form 5291 may be used; alternatively, any format acceptable if terms are as explicit as Form 5291
- **Scope:** May be general (all customs business) or limited (specified part of business)
- **Duration:** Partnership POA limited to 2 years from execution. All others may be unlimited duration (19 CFR 141.34).
- **Execution:** Must be signed by an officer of the corporation (for corporate importers)
- **Nonresident principals:** POA not accepted unless the designated agent is a U.S. resident authorized to accept service of process

**Record Retention:**
- Active POAs: retained until revoked
- Revoked POAs and revocation letters: retained for 5 years after revocation or 5 years after client ceases to be "active," whichever is later

**Important:** Payment to a customs broker does not relieve the Importer of Record of liability for customs charges, duties, taxes, or other debts owed to CBP.

## PART 2: PGA-SPECIFIC DOCUMENTS

### 2.1 FDA (Food and Drug Administration)

**Legal Authority:** Public Health Security and Bioterrorism Preparedness and Response Act of 2002; FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) of 2011; 21 CFR Part 1

**Prior Notice of Imported Food:**
- Required for all food (including animal feed) imported or offered for import into the U.S.
- Filed through FDA's Prior Notice System Interface (PNSI) or CBP's Automated Broker Interface (ABI/ACS)
- **Maximum advance filing:** 15 calendar days before anticipated arrival (PNSI); 30 calendar days (ABI/ACS)
- **Minimum advance filing:** 
  - 2 hours before arrival by land/road
  - 4 hours before arrival by air or land/rail
  - 8 hours before arrival by water
- FSMA amendment (2011): Must report any country to which the article has been refused entry
- Non-compliance: food subject to refusal of admission and holding; civil or criminal penalties; CBP may seize merchandise

**Food Facility Registration (21 CFR 1.225-1.243):**
- Domestic and foreign facilities that manufacture, process, pack, or hold food for human/animal consumption in the U.S. must register with FDA
- Food from unregistered foreign facilities subject to holding at port until registration completed
- FSMA amended registration requirements; biennial renewal required
- Must designate a U.S. agent (for foreign facilities)

**FDA Registration for Drugs, Devices, Cosmetics:**
- Drug establishments: registration under 21 CFR Part 207
- Medical devices: premarket approval (PMA) or 510(k) clearance; establishment registration under 21 CFR Part 807
- Cosmetics: voluntary registration (mandatory reporting under MoCRA, effective 2024)

### 2.2 USDA/APHIS (Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service)

**Legal Authority:** Plant Protection Act (7 U.S.C. 7701-7786); Animal Health Protection Act (7 U.S.C. 8301-8320); ISPM 15

**Phytosanitary Certificates:**
- Required for plant and plant product imports to certify freedom from quarantine pests
- Issued by the National Plant Protection Organization (NPPO) of the exporting country
- Must conform to International Plant Protection Convention (IPPC) model certificate
- Electronic copies accepted, but inspectors may request originals

**Veterinary Certificates / Health Certificates:**
- Required for live animal imports and certain animal products
- Issued by the official veterinary authority of the exporting country
- Must certify animal health status and freedom from specified diseases

**APHIS Licenses, Permits, Certificates (LPCOs):**
- Permits for regulated plants, animals, and organisms
- CITES certificates for listed species
- Certificates of origin for certain agricultural products
- Required manufacturer statements
- Filed in ACE via APHIS Core Message Set

**Wood Packaging Material:**
- Must be ISPM 15 compliant (heat treated or methyl bromide fumigated)
- Must bear the proper IPPC/ISPM 15 stamp/mark
- Non-compliant wood packaging subject to reshipment, treatment, or destruction

**Lacey Act Declarations (administered by APHIS):**
- **Legal Authority:** Lacey Act of 1900 (as amended 2008); 16 U.S.C. 3371-3378
- Required for imported plants and plant products (wood, paper, furniture, etc.)
- **Phase VII** (effective December 1, 2024): covers virtually all remaining plant product HTS codes
- **Required information:** (a) scientific name (genus and species), (b) country of harvest, (c) quantity
- Filed via ACE or APHIS LAWGS system
- "Due care" standard requires importers to investigate and ensure legal sourcing
- Penalties: $250 administrative penalty for unknowing violations; civil/criminal penalties for knowing violations

### 2.3 EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)

**Legal Authority:** Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA), 15 U.S.C. 2601-2697; Clean Air Act (vehicle emissions)

**TSCA Certification:**
- Required for importing chemicals in bulk, mixtures, or as part of articles
- Importer must file a positive or negative TSCA certification statement at time of entry
- Positive certification: chemical substance is on the TSCA Inventory and complies with all TSCA requirements
- Negative certification: chemical is not subject to TSCA (e.g., pesticides, food additives regulated by other statutes)
- New chemical substances require Pre-Manufacture Notice (PMN) filed at least 90 days before import

**TSCA Title VI (Composite Wood Products):**
- Import certification required for hardwood plywood, particleboard, MDF, thin-MDF
- Importers must retain bills of lading, invoices, and supplier compliance statements for 3 years

**Vehicle/Engine Compliance:**
- EPA Form 3520-1 required for imported vehicles
- Declares emissions compliance under Clean Air Act
- Must be filed alongside DOT Form HS-7

### 2.4 CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission)

**Legal Authority:** Consumer Product Safety Act (CPSA), 15 U.S.C. 2051-2089; Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act of 2008 (CPSIA); 16 CFR Part 1110

**General Certificate of Conformity (GCC):**
- Required for general-use consumer products subject to a consumer product safety rule
- Must be issued by the domestic manufacturer or importer
- Based on testing or a reasonable testing program

**Children's Product Certificate (CPC):**
- Required for children's products (designed/intended primarily for children 12 and under)
- Must be based on testing by a CPSC-accepted, accredited third-party laboratory

**eFiling Requirement (Final Rule, January 8, 2025):**
- Importers must electronically file certificates of compliance with CBP at time of entry
- **Effective date: July 8, 2026**
- Seven data elements: product ID, citation codes, manufacture date, manufacture place, test date, testing lab, point of contact

### 2.5 FCC (Federal Communications Commission)

**Legal Authority:** Section 302 of the Communications Act; 47 CFR Part 2 (Equipment Authorization)

**Two Authorization Procedures:**

| Procedure | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Supplier's Declaration of Conformity (SDoC)** | Responsible party (must be U.S.-based) ensures equipment complies with technical standards. No FCC application required. Not listed in FCC database. Test report available on request. |
| **Certification** | Equipment tested by FCC-recognized accredited lab. Responsible party obtains FCC Registration Number (FRN). Equipment listed in FCC database. Required for intentional radiators and most restricted devices. |

**Importation (47 CFR 2.1204):** RF equipment imports must meet one of eleven conditions: (1) have required FCC authorization; (2) imported for evaluation; (3) imported for trade show demonstration; or (4-11) other permitted conditions.

Equipment without proper authorization may be detained at the border. FCC works with CBP to enforce equipment authorization requirements.

### 2.6 TTB (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau)

**Legal Authority:** Federal Alcohol Administration Act (27 U.S.C. 201-212); 27 CFR Part 1

**Federal Basic Importer's Permit:**
- Required for any person importing distilled spirits, wine, or malt beverages
- Application: TTB Form 5100.24 filed with TTB's National Revenue Center
- No fee charged; importer must maintain a staffed U.S. business office
- Current permits are inclusive of all three commodities (wine, beer, spirits)

**Certificate of Label Approval (COLA):**
- Required for each unique product/label before importation
- Application: TTB Form 5100.31 filed with Alcohol Labeling and Formulation Division
- Must be in possession at time of importation
- Labels must comply with TTB regulations for the specific beverage type

### 2.7 U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service

**Legal Authority:** Endangered Species Act; CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species); Lacey Act; 50 CFR Parts 13-23

**CITES Permits:**
- Required for import/export of any CITES-listed species (live specimens, parts, products, pets)
- **Appendix I:** Species threatened with extinction; commercial trade prohibited with limited exemptions
- **Appendix II:** Species not presently threatened but may become so; trade regulated
- **Appendix III:** Species protected in at least one country requesting CITES cooperation

**Import/Export Requirements:**
- Wildlife must enter/exit through a **Designated U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service Port** unless a Designated Port Exception Permit is obtained
- Live or perishable wildlife: **48-hour advance notice** of estimated arrival time required
- Permit applications: allow **60-90 days** for review
- FWS Form 3-177 (Declaration for Importation or Exportation of Fish or Wildlife) required for all commercial wildlife shipments

**Lacey Act (wildlife provisions):**
- Prohibits trade in illegally sourced wildlife
- Declaration requirements for plants and plant products administered by APHIS (see Section 2.2)

### 2.8 DOT/NHTSA (National Highway Traffic Safety Administration)

**Legal Authority:** National Traffic and Motor Vehicle Safety Act (49 U.S.C. 30101-30170); 49 CFR Part 591-594

**HS-7 Declaration Form:**
- Required at time of import for all motor vehicles and motor vehicle equipment
- Declares whether vehicle complies with Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards (FMVSS), Bumper Standards, and Theft Prevention Standards
- Filed alongside EPA Form 3520-1 (emissions)

**Key HS-7 Declaration Boxes:**

| Box | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Box 1 | Vehicle conforms to all applicable FMVSS at time of manufacture (OEM import) |
| Box 2A/2B | Vehicle does not conform but will be brought into compliance by a DOT Registered Importer (RI) |
| Box 3 | Vehicle is 25+ years old (exempt from FMVSS) |
| Box 7 | Temporary importation (research, demonstrations, racing) -- NHTSA permission letter needed unless certified manufacturer |
| Box 8 | Vehicle not primarily manufactured for public road use (off-highway) -- exempt from NHTSA jurisdiction |

**Nonconforming Vehicles:**
- Bond required: **150% of entered value**
- Must be brought into conformity within **120 days**
- Registered Importer submits statement of conformity to NHTSA
- If not conformed: must be exported or abandoned to the U.S.

| **CFR** | Cost and Freight | Sea/inland waterway | On board vessel (origin) | **Seller** | **Buyer** | **Buyer** | Named port of destination | None |
| **CIF** | Cost, Insurance & Freight | Sea/inland waterway | On board vessel (origin) | **Seller** | **Buyer** | **Buyer** | Named port of destination | **Seller** (Clause C - minimum, 110% value) |

### 3.2 Detailed Definitions

**GROUP 1: Rules for Any Mode of Transport**

**EXW -- Ex Works (named place of delivery)**
- Seller delivers goods at its premises (or another named place). Seller need not load goods or clear for export.
- Buyer bears ALL costs and risks from that point: loading, inland transport, export clearance, main carriage, import clearance, duties, delivery.
- EXW is the only Incoterm where the buyer handles export clearance -- problematic if buyer is not established in seller's country.
- Customs valuation impact: EXW price excludes all transport. For U.S. FOB valuation basis, foreign inland freight to port of export should be added to arrive at customs value (though per 19 CFR 152.103, ex-factory prices do not require mandatory addition of foreign inland freight unless verification reveals overstated charges).

**FCA -- Free Carrier (named place of delivery)**
- Seller delivers goods to a carrier or named place: (a) at seller's premises, loaded onto collecting vehicle; or (b) at another place (terminal, warehouse), not unloaded from seller's vehicle.
- Risk transfers when goods are handed to the first carrier.
- Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles import clearance and duties.
- **Incoterms 2020 innovation:** Parties can agree that buyer instructs carrier to issue on-board B/L to seller (critical for L/C transactions with containerized cargo).
- Customs valuation impact: FCA price typically includes inland transport to carrier pickup point and export clearance costs. For CIF-basis countries, ocean freight and insurance must be added.

**CPT -- Carriage Paid To (named place of destination)**
- Seller contracts and pays for carriage to the named place of destination. Risk transfers when goods are handed to the first carrier (NOT at destination).
- Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles import clearance and duties.
- Key distinction: seller pays transport to destination, but risk transfers at origin when goods are with the carrier.
- Customs valuation impact: CPT price includes freight to destination. For U.S. FOB-basis valuation, international freight must be deducted (using actual costs).

**CIP -- Carriage and Insurance Paid To (named place of destination)**
- Same as CPT, plus seller must procure insurance.
- **Incoterms 2020 change:** CIP requires Institute Cargo Clauses (A) / "all risks" coverage at minimum 110% of contract value (increased from Clause C in 2010 rules).
- Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles import clearance and duties.
- Customs valuation impact: CIP price includes freight and insurance. For U.S. FOB-basis valuation, both international freight and insurance must be deducted.

**DAP -- Delivered at Place (named place of destination)**
- Seller delivers goods to named place in buyer's country, ready for unloading. Seller bears all costs and risks until that point.
- Buyer unloads, clears customs for import, pays duties and taxes.
- Customs valuation impact: DAP price includes all transport to destination. For U.S. FOB-basis valuation, deduct international freight, insurance, and any post-importation inland transport in the U.S.

**DPU -- Delivered at Place Unloaded (named place of destination)**
- Seller delivers and unloads goods at named place. Risk transfers after unloading.
- Replaced DAT (Delivered at Terminal) from Incoterms 2010 -- DPU can be any place, not just a terminal.
- Buyer handles import clearance and duties.
- Customs valuation impact: DPU price includes all transport and unloading. For U.S. FOB-basis, deduct international freight, insurance, and unloading/inland costs.

**DDP -- Delivered Duty Paid (named place of destination)**
- Maximum obligation on seller. Seller delivers goods to named place, cleared for import, with all duties and taxes paid.
- **Only Incoterm where seller is responsible for import clearance and duties.**
- Buyer only responsible for unloading at final destination.
- Risk: Seller may need to be a registered entity for import/VAT in buyer's country.
- Customs valuation impact: DDP price includes everything -- transport, insurance, duties, taxes. For customs declaration purposes, the duty amount and post-importation charges should be backed out to arrive at dutiable value.

**GROUP 2: Rules for Sea and Inland Waterway Transport Only**

**FAS -- Free Alongside Ship (named port of shipment)**
- Seller delivers goods, cleared for export, alongside the vessel at named port.
- Risk transfers when goods are alongside ship.
- Buyer loads goods onto vessel, contracts main carriage, handles import clearance, pays duties.
- Customs valuation impact: FAS price includes inland transport to port and export clearance. For U.S. FOB-basis, loading charges onto vessel may need to be added. For CIF-basis countries, add loading, ocean freight, and insurance.

**FOB -- Free On Board (named port of shipment)**
- Seller delivers goods, cleared for export, loaded on board the vessel.
- Risk transfers when goods are on board the vessel.
- Buyer contracts main carriage, pays ocean freight, handles import clearance, pays duties.
- Customs valuation impact: FOB price aligns most closely with U.S. customs valuation basis. In the U.S., FOB value is generally the dutiable value with no major adjustments needed for freight/insurance.

**CFR -- Cost and Freight (named port of destination)**
- Seller delivers goods on board vessel (risk transfers at loading). Seller also contracts and pays for freight to named port of destination.
- Critical: risk and cost split at different points -- risk at loading (origin), cost at destination.
- Buyer handles import clearance and duties. No insurance obligation on seller.
- Customs valuation impact: CFR price includes international freight. For U.S. FOB-basis, international freight must be deducted from declared value.

**CIF -- Cost, Insurance and Freight (named port of destination)**
- Same as CFR, plus seller contracts for marine insurance.
- **Incoterms 2020:** CIF retains minimum Institute Cargo Clauses (C) at 110% of contract value (unlike CIP which upgraded to Clause A).
- Risk transfers when goods are loaded on board vessel (not at destination).
- Seller must provide buyer with invoice, insurance policy, and bill of lading (the three core CIF documents).
- Customs valuation impact: CIF price includes both freight and insurance. For U.S. FOB-basis, both must be deducted. For CIF-basis countries (India, EU), CIF price is the dutiable value.

### 3.3 Incoterms Impact on Customs Valuation (U.S. FOB Basis)

The United States values goods on an **FOB basis** per 19 U.S.C. 1401a(b)(4)(A), meaning international freight, insurance, and related charges are **excluded** from dutiable value. The Incoterm on the invoice tells CBP what is or is not already included in the price:

| Incoterm | Adjustments Needed for U.S. Customs Value (FOB Basis) |
|----------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| **EXW** | Price excludes all transport. Foreign inland freight to port of export is NOT required to be added (ex-factory prices accepted per 19 CFR 152.103). However, if inland freight is included and overstated, CBP may adjust. |
| **FCA** | Depends on named place. If named place is inland, no adjustment typically needed. If includes port charges, review for inclusion of international elements. |
| **FAS** | May need to add loading charges (vessel loading costs) if considered part of export-side value. |
| **FOB** | **No adjustment typically needed** -- aligns with U.S. valuation basis. |
| **CFR** | **Deduct** international ocean freight (must be actual cost, supported by freight invoice or rated B/L). |
| **CIF** | **Deduct** international ocean freight AND insurance (must be actual costs with supporting documentation). |
| **CPT** | **Deduct** international freight portion (actual cost). |
| **CIP** | **Deduct** international freight AND insurance (actual costs). |
| **DAP** | **Deduct** international freight, insurance, and any U.S. domestic transport included in price. |
| **DPU** | **Deduct** international freight, insurance, unloading costs, and any U.S. domestic transport. |
| **DDP** | **Deduct** international freight, insurance, U.S. domestic transport, import duties, and taxes from the price to arrive at dutiable value. |

**Critical requirement:** All deductions must be based on **actual costs**, not estimates. CBP requires actual freight invoices, rated bills of lading, or freight contracts. Estimated costs cannot be deducted. CBP can request proof for the past 5 years and all future entries (per CBP Informed Compliance Publication on Proper Deductions).

### 3.4 Most Commonly Used Incoterms by Transport Mode

| Transport Mode | Most Common Incoterms | Notes |
|---------------|----------------------|-------|
| **Ocean (containerized)** | FCA, CIF, FOB, CFR | ICC recommends FCA over FOB for containers (risk transfers before container enters terminal). CIF dominant in commodity trading. |
| **Ocean (bulk/break-bulk)** | FOB, CIF, CFR, FAS | Traditional sea terms most appropriate for non-containerized cargo where goods can be verified at shipside. |
| **Air freight** | FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, EXW | Sea-only terms (FOB, CIF, CFR, FAS) should NOT be used for air shipments. FCA is the air-appropriate analog of FOB. |
| **Multimodal / Road / Rail** | FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DDP | Any-mode terms. FCA most common for road/rail. DDP growing for e-commerce. |

## PART 4: CUSTOMS VALUATION

### 4.1 Legal Framework

**Primary Law:** 19 U.S.C. 1401a (Trade Agreements Act of 1979, Title II)
**Implementing Regulations:** 19 CFR Part 152, Subpart E
**International Framework:** WTO Agreement on Implementation of Article VII of GATT 1994 (Customs Valuation Agreement)

The U.S. system conforms to the WTO Valuation Agreement, which establishes a fair, uniform, and neutral system based on commercial realities, outlawing arbitrary or fictitious values.

### 4.2 The Six Valuation Methods (in Hierarchical Order)

Methods must be applied in the following order. Only if a method cannot be used does one proceed to the next. The only exception: Methods 4 and 5 can be reversed at the importer's request (not at customs' discretion).

| Priority | Method | Legal Basis | Description |
|----------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **1** | **Transaction Value** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(b) | Price actually paid or payable for the goods when sold for exportation to the U.S., plus statutory additions. Primary and most commonly used method (~90% of entries). |
| **2** | **Transaction Value of Identical Goods** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(c) | Transaction value of identical goods sold for export to the U.S. at or about the same time, at same commercial level and in substantially the same quantity. |
| **3** | **Transaction Value of Similar Goods** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(c) | Transaction value of similar goods (same function, commercially interchangeable) exported at or about the same time. |
| **4** | **Deductive Value** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(d) | Based on unit price at which imported (or identical/similar) goods are sold in the U.S. in greatest aggregate quantity to unrelated buyers, minus deductions for commissions, profit, transport, insurance, duties/taxes, and value added by further processing. |
| **5** | **Computed Value** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(e) | Based on cost of materials + fabrication/processing + profit and general expenses equal to those usually reflected in sales of goods of the same class/kind from the country of export. |
| **6** | **Fall-Back Method** | 19 U.S.C. 1401a(f) | Value derived from Methods 1-5, applied with reasonable flexibility. Cannot use: domestic selling prices, higher of two alternatives, price in export country's domestic market, cost of production other than computed values, prices for export to third countries, minimum customs values, or arbitrary/fictitious values. |

### 4.3 Transaction Value: Additions (19 CFR 152.103(b))

The following amounts are added to the price actually paid or payable (and NO others):

1. **Packing costs** incurred by the buyer -- the cost of all containers and coverings of whatever nature, and of packing, whether for labor or materials, used in placing the merchandise in condition, packed ready for shipment to the United States

2. **Selling commissions** incurred by the buyer -- commissions paid to the seller's agent who is related to or controlled by, or works for or on behalf of, the manufacturer or the seller

3. **Assists** (19 CFR 152.103(d)) -- the value, apportioned as appropriate, of any assist. An "assist" includes:
   - Materials, components, parts, or similar items incorporated in the imported goods
   - Tools, dies, molds, or similar items used in production
   - Merchandise consumed in production
   - Engineering, development, artwork, design work, plans, and sketches undertaken elsewhere than in the United States and necessary for production
   
   **Assist valuation:** Cost of acquisition (from unrelated seller) or cost of production (if produced by buyer or related party), including transportation costs to place of production. Receiving inspection and warehouse costs are NOT part of assist value.
   
   **Apportionment methods:** (a) over first shipment, (b) over units produced to date, (c) over entire anticipated production, or (d) other method per generally accepted accounting principles.

4. **Royalties and license fees** -- related to the imported merchandise that the buyer is required to pay, directly or indirectly, as a **condition of the sale** for exportation to the U.S. Key test: could the buyer purchase the merchandise without paying the fee? If not, it is dutiable.
   - Royalties based on post-importation activities may or may not be dutiable depending on whether they are a condition of the sale
   - Trademark fees per-unit paid to the seller: included
   - Royalties paid to unrelated third parties: included if payment is a condition of the sale

5. **Proceeds** -- of any subsequent resale, disposal, or use of the imported merchandise that accrue, directly or indirectly, to the seller

### 4.4 Transaction Value: Exclusions and Deductions

**Excluded from transaction value** per 19 U.S.C. 1401a(b)(4)(A) and 19 CFR 152.103(i):

1. **International transportation costs** -- freight, insurance, and related services incident to the international shipment from the country of exportation to the place of importation in the U.S. (The U.S. values on an FOB basis)
2. **U.S. domestic inland freight** -- costs of transporting goods after importation, if identified separately
3. **Post-importation charges** -- reasonable costs for construction, erection, assembly, maintenance, or technical assistance after importation, if accurately identifiable as separate from the price
4. **Customs duties and federal taxes** -- prepaid U.S. customs duties and federal excise taxes, if identified separately

**Critical rules for claiming deductions:**
- Must use **actual costs**, not estimates (long-standing CBP position)
- Must have documentation: actual freight invoice from carrier, rated B/L, or freight contract
- CBP can request proof at any time, looking back 5 years
- If actual costs unavailable at time of entry, pay duty on full value and file post-summary correction when actual costs obtained

**Foreign Inland Freight:** If the price is ex-factory (does not include foreign inland freight), those charges are NOT added to transaction value. However, CBP may adjust if foreign inland freight has been overstated.

**Post-importation rebates:** Any rebate or decrease in price between buyer and seller after the date of importation is disregarded for transaction value purposes.

### 4.5 The First Sale Rule

**Legal Basis:** 19 U.S.C. 1401a(b)(1); T.D. 96-87 (December 13, 1996)

In a multi-tiered transaction (e.g., foreign manufacturer sells to foreign middleman, who sells to U.S. importer), the customs value can be based on the **first sale** (manufacturer to middleman) rather than the **last sale** (middleman to importer), provided:

**Requirements:**
1. The first sale must be a bona fide arm's-length sale
2. The goods must have been "sold for exportation to the United States" at the time of the first sale
3. The goods must not have been substantially transformed between the first and last sale
4. The importer bears the burden of proof

**Benefits:** The first sale price is typically lower than the final sale price, reducing the dutiable value and therefore duties owed.

**Documentation required:**
- Purchase orders, contracts, invoices for each sale in the chain
- Evidence that goods were destined for the U.S. at time of first sale
- Proof that first sale price is arm's-length (especially if related parties involved)
- Evidence no substantial transformation occurred after first sale

**CBP's proposed reinterpretation (2008 Federal Register):** CBP proposed that transaction value should be based on the **last sale** before introduction into the U.S. This proposed rule was never finalized, and the first sale rule remains available, but importers should monitor regulatory developments.

### 4.6 Related Party Transactions

**Legal Basis:** 19 U.S.C. 1401a(b)(2)(B); 19 U.S.C. 1401a(g)

**Definition of "Related" (19 U.S.C. 1401a(g)):** Parties are related when:
- Members of the same family
- Officers or directors of one another's businesses
- Legally recognized partners
- Employer and employee
- Any person directly or indirectly owning, controlling, or holding 5%+ of voting shares
- Two or more persons directly or indirectly controlled by a third person
- Buyer and seller who are one and the same person

**When related parties are involved:**
- Must be reported on Entry Summary (Form 7501, Block 34)
- Transaction value may still be used if one of two tests is satisfied:

**Test 1 -- Circumstances of Sale:** Examination demonstrates that the relationship did not influence the price. Factors include: how the parties organize their commercial relations, how the price was derived, whether the price is consistent with normal pricing practices, whether it is sufficient to cover costs and provide a profit consistent with overall profits over a representative period.

**Test 2 -- Test Values:** The transaction value closely approximates one of the following previously accepted values:
- Transaction value of identical goods in sales to unrelated buyers
- Deductive value of identical or similar goods
- Computed value of identical or similar goods

### 4.7 Comprehensive Valuation Adjustments by Incoterm

This table synthesizes how to move from the invoice price (based on the stated Incoterm) to the U.S. customs value (FOB basis):

| Incoterm | Price Includes | Add to Arrive at Customs Value | Deduct to Arrive at Customs Value |
|----------|---------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **EXW** | Goods at seller's premises only | Nothing required (ex-factory accepted). But verify if assists, royalties, proceeds should be added per 19 CFR 152.103(b). | N/A |
| **FCA** | Goods + inland transport to named carrier + export clearance | Verify all 5 statutory additions (assists, royalties, etc.) | If named place includes any international transport element, deduct that portion. |
| **FAS** | Goods + inland transport + export clearance + delivery alongside ship | Verify statutory additions | N/A (loading not included) |
| **FOB** | Goods + inland transport + export clearance + loading on vessel | Verify statutory additions | N/A -- FOB aligns with U.S. valuation basis |
| **CFR** | FOB + international ocean freight | Verify statutory additions | International ocean freight (actual cost, documented) |
| **CIF** | FOB + international ocean freight + insurance | Verify statutory additions | International freight + insurance (both actual costs, documented) |
| **CPT** | FCA + international freight to destination | Verify statutory additions | International freight portion (actual cost) |
| **CIP** | FCA + international freight + insurance to destination | Verify statutory additions | International freight + insurance (actual costs) |
| **DAP** | All transport to destination (excluding unloading, import clearance, duties) | Verify statutory additions | International freight + insurance + any U.S. inland transport (all actual costs) |
| **DPU** | All transport + unloading at destination (excluding import clearance, duties) | Verify statutory additions | International freight + insurance + unloading + U.S. inland transport (all actual costs) |
| **DDP** | Everything including import duties and taxes | Verify statutory additions | International freight + insurance + U.S. inland transport + import duties + taxes (all actual costs) |
