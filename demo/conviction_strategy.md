# The Conviction Strategy

## Designing the Experience That Funds a Moonshot

---

> **Purpose:** This document designs the complete set of experiences, artifacts, and encounters that take an executive or funder from "interesting idea" to "I'm writing the check." The interactive demo is one tool. It is not the only tool, and for some audiences, it is not the right first tool. This document designs the full system.

---

## The Problem We're Solving (Not the Customs Problem — the Conviction Problem)

The customs vision is sound. The knowledge base is rigorous. The partnership logic is clear. But none of that matters if the people with the authority and budget to greenlight this don't *feel it in their bones*.

Executives and funders don't make moonshot commitments because a document convinced them logically. They make them because:

1. **They felt the pain personally** — not as a statistic, but as a story they recognized.
2. **They experienced the future** — not described to them, but shown to them in a way they could touch.
3. **They believed the team could execute** — not because of a resume, but because they saw evidence of capability.
4. **They felt the window closing** — not a vague sense of urgency, but a specific competitive threat with a timeline.
5. **They could see themselves explaining it** — to their board, their peers, their team. If they can't retell it simply, they won't champion it.

This strategy designs five distinct experiences, each optimized for a different audience and moment. They work independently and reinforce each other.

---

## The Five Experiences

| Experience | Format | Audience | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. The Encounter** | In-person narrative + artifact | C-suite, 1-on-1 or small group | 15-20 min | Plant the seed. Make them curious enough to ask for more. |
| **2. The Golden Path** | Interactive live demo | Decision-makers, 10-30 people | 45-60 min | Make the future tangible. Create desire. |
| **3. The War Game** | Facilitated simulation | Operating leaders + their teams | Half day | Make the urgency real. Show what happens if we don't act. |
| **4. The Deep Dive** | Data room + working session | Finance, strategy, technology diligence | 2-3 hours | Answer every hard question. Prove the economics. |
| **5. The Retelling Kit** | Leave-behind artifacts | Anyone who needs to champion internally | Async | Give them the tools to sell it when you're not in the room. |

Each is designed below.

---

## Experience 1: The Encounter

### What It Is

A 15-20 minute, low-tech, narrative-driven conversation designed for the first meeting with a C-level executive or senior funder. No slides. No laptop. No demo. Just a story, a single physical artifact, and a question.

### Why It Exists

You do not get 60 minutes with a CEO on the first meeting. You get 15-20, usually sandwiched between other things, often in a hallway, a dinner, or an informal pull-aside. The Encounter is designed for this moment. Its only goal is to create enough curiosity that the executive says: "Show me more." That's it. Not conviction. Not commitment. Curiosity.

### The Design

**The Story (5 minutes):**

The presenter tells one story. Not the whole vision. One story. Choose the one that matches the executive's world:

- **For a logistics/operations executive:** The story of 4 million parcels. "On August twenty-ninth, we woke up to four million new formal entries a day. Our systems were built for a fraction of that. Our cages are at a hundred and twenty percent capacity in Memphis. Every hold takes four to seven days to resolve because the process runs on emails, phone calls, and fax machines. This is not a future problem. This is a this-morning problem."

- **For a revenue/commercial executive:** The story of unclaimed savings. "Fifteen to thirty percent of trade that qualifies for duty-free treatment under USMCA, KORUS, and other trade agreements doesn't claim it. Billions of dollars a year in savings that importers are legally entitled to — sitting on the table. Nobody picks it up because the qualification analysis is complex and compliance teams are drowning in holds. What if we picked it up for them — and took a share?"

- **For a technology/digital executive:** The story of the declaration dying. "Accenture has been telling customs authorities around the world that the customs declaration is dead. The EU is building a system where customs authorities pull data from commercial data streams — invoices, purchase orders, logistics bookings — instead of requiring a separate declaration. CBP's 21st Century Customs Framework moves in the same direction. The company that builds the commercial platform that feeds these systems owns the interface between global commerce and government. That's a strategic position, not a product feature."

- **For a funder/investor:** The story of the whitespace. "DHL has the best compliance tools. Amazon has the best classification AI. Flexport has the best visibility. Singapore has the best government system. Nobody has put them all together. The customs brokerage market is fifty-two billion dollars. The unclaimed FTA savings are twenty to forty billion. The de minimis elimination just created millions of new entries that need automated processing. And no one has built the platform."

**The Artifact (3 minutes):**

One physical object. Not a deck. Not a document. An object that the executive can hold, look at, and keep.

**Option A: The Tariff Card.** A high-quality printed card (think: heavy card stock, beautiful typography, the size of a large postcard) showing the tariff stacking example:

```
Consumer Electronics from China

MFN Duty                 6.0%
Section 301            + 7.5%
IEEPA Reciprocal      +60.0%
─────────────────────────────
Total Effective Rate    73.5%

On a $45 wireless router, the consumer
pays $38.25 in duty — more than the
cost of the product itself.
```

On the back:

```
The same product from Mexico under USMCA:

Total Effective Rate    0.0%

Same product. Different origin.
The platform that makes this calculation
instantly, at the point of sale, for every
product in the world — doesn't exist yet.
```

The card is designed to sit on a desk. It is a conversation piece. When someone visits the executive's office and asks "what's that?", the executive retells the story. The card does the work when you're not in the room.

**Option B: The Before/After Receipt.** Two receipts printed on actual receipt paper (thermal paper, slightly curled, the way a real receipt looks). One shows the "Today" experience: $50.40 at checkout, then a separate $7.43 duty bill five days later. The other shows the "Tomorrow" experience: $57.83 all-inclusive, delivered in three days. Same total. Different experience. Stapled together.

This is visceral. Everyone has held a receipt. The format is instantly legible. And the before/after contrast — same money, completely different experience — is the vision reduced to its essence.

**Option C: The Dashboard Screenshot.** A single, beautifully printed screenshot of David's dashboard (Screen 2A from the demo design) on high-quality paper. In the corner, a handwritten note: "4,847 entries. 98.1% cleared overnight. 3 strategic alerts found $4.7M in savings and prevented a UFLPA detention. This is what Monday morning looks like."

The handwritten note is important. It says "this is personal, not a mass-produced brochure."

**The Question (2 minutes):**

End the Encounter with a question, not an ask. The ask comes later. The question is:

"If this platform existed today — if your shippers saw landed cost at the point of sale, if your compliance teams managed by exception instead of by transaction, if holds resolved in hours instead of days — what would that be worth to [FedEx / your portfolio / this business unit]?"

Do not answer the question. Let the executive answer it. Their answer tells you how they frame value, what they care about, and how to structure the next conversation. And the act of answering the question begins their ownership of the idea.

**Close:**

"I'd like to show you what it looks like. Can I get an hour with your team?"

That's the only output the Encounter needs to produce: a follow-up meeting.

### Artifact Production Notes

- **The Tariff Card:** Design in Figma or Illustrator. Print on 350gsm uncoated stock, 6"×4". Matte finish. Typography: the same font family as the demo (Inter or brand font). Color: minimal — dark text on white, with the total rate in FedEx orange. No logos on the front. A small, tasteful FedEx logo on the back. The card should feel like it was designed by someone who cares about typography, not by a marketing department. That signals quality and intentionality.

- **The Receipts:** Source actual thermal receipt paper and a thermal printer. Print the two receipts with realistic formatting (store name: "Lisbon Ceramics", date, item, subtotals). The "duty bill" receipt should look like a separate document — different header, carrier name, "AMOUNT DUE" in caps. The contrast between the messy two-receipt experience and the clean single-receipt experience is the point. Package them in a small envelope or clip them together.

- **The Dashboard Screenshot:** Print on A4 or Letter, matte photo paper. The screenshot should be from the actual demo build (once it's complete) — this creates continuity between the Encounter and the subsequent Golden Path demo. The handwritten note should be actually handwritten, not a handwriting font. This matters.

**Produce 20-30 of the chosen artifact.** They're cheap and they work long after the meeting ends.

## Experience 3: The War Game

### What It Is

A facilitated half-day simulation where operating leaders and their teams play through two scenarios: (1) what happens to their business in 2027-2028 if the clearance status quo continues, and (2) what happens if the platform exists. They don't watch a presentation. They make decisions, face consequences, and discover the answers themselves.

### Why It Exists

Demos create desire. War games create urgency. A demo shows the future you want. A war game shows the future you'll get if you don't act. For operating leaders — the VPs and SVPs who run clearance operations, brokerage, international shipping, and e-commerce — the most compelling argument is not "look at this beautiful platform" but "look at what happens to your P&L and your operations if DHL builds this first."

War games also create organizational buy-in that demos cannot. When a team collectively discovers the problem and the solution through simulation, they own it. They don't need to be sold. They've already sold themselves.

### The Design

**Participants:** 12-20 people. Mix of:
- Clearance operations leaders
- Brokerage leadership
- International e-commerce/commercial leaders
- Technology/platform leaders
- Finance (1-2 people who understand the P&L)
- Strategy (1-2 people who understand competitive positioning)

**Duration:** 4 hours (half day), with one break.

**Facilitation:** Two facilitators. One runs the simulation. One tracks decisions and outcomes on a visible board/screen.

---

**Hour 1: The Scenario Setup (45 min)**

The facilitator presents "the world in 2028" — not the FedEx vision, but the *market reality* that will exist regardless of what FedEx does:

- US de minimis has been eliminated for 3 years. EU de minimis eliminated for 2 years. UK eliminated for 1 year. Thailand, Indonesia, India already eliminated.
- IEEPA tariffs have been modified 6 more times since 2026. Three new country-specific tariff programs have been introduced.
- UFLPA enforcement has expanded to 4 new commodity categories. EU CBAM is in its definitive phase. EUDR is fully enforced.
- The EU Customs Data Hub Phase 1 is operational. CBP has issued the RFP for ACE 2.0.
- E-commerce cross-border volume has grown 40% since 2026.
- Two of the three major express carriers have deployed AI classification covering 80%+ of their entry volume.
- A major e-commerce platform has built its own customs clearance capability and is offering it to third-party sellers.

**None of these are predictions.** Every one is a straight-line projection from documented trends in the knowledge base. The facilitator presents them as facts, not scenarios.

The facilitator then divides the room into three teams:

- **Team Alpha:** You are FedEx. You have not invested in a clearance platform. You are operating with today's systems and workforce, incrementally improved.
- **Team Beta:** You are FedEx. You invested in the clearance platform in 2026. It is now in Phase 3 (Predict and Prevent). 92% STP. Landed cost APIs live. FTA auto-qualification generating $500M in annual gain-share revenue.
- **Team Gamma:** You are DHL. You invested aggressively in TAS expansion and a new clearance AI platform in 2026. You are ahead of FedEx on customer-facing trade tools and are beginning to capture FedEx's enterprise customers.

**Hour 2: The War Game Rounds (90 min)**

Three rounds, each presenting a crisis or opportunity. Each team has 15 minutes to decide how they respond. The facilitator plays the role of the market — announcing events, delivering customer reactions, and quantifying consequences.

**Round 1: "The Tariff Shock"**

> "IEEPA reciprocal tariffs on the EU increase from 0% to 25% overnight, effective in 72 hours. You have 400,000 shipments in transit to the US from EU origins. Every one needs its duty recalculated. Your enterprise importers are calling, demanding to know their new landed cost. Three major e-commerce platforms are asking if you can update their duty calculations before the effective date."

Each team has 15 minutes to present their response:
- **Team Alpha (no platform):** Manually update rate tables? How long? Which shipments are affected? Can you even identify them? How do you communicate with 400,000 shippers?
- **Team Beta (with platform):** The regulatory intelligence engine updates the rate. The system identifies every affected shipment. Landed cost APIs automatically reflect the new rates. Shippers on the platform see updated pricing within hours. In-transit shipments are proactively re-quoted.
- **Team Gamma (DHL):** DHL's TAS updates their compliance screening. But do their customers see the update at the point of sale? How quickly? What about the customers who don't use TAS?

The facilitator then announces the *consequence*: "Two of your top-10 enterprise customers — $200M combined annual clearance revenue — have been approached by [the team with the best response] with a pitch showing how they handled the tariff shock. One of them has agreed to a trial."

**Round 2: "The UFLPA Expansion"**

> "The Forced Labor Enforcement Task Force adds polyester and polyester-blend textiles to the UFLPA priority enforcement sector. Effective immediately, all polyester-containing apparel imports from Southeast Asia are subject to enhanced UFLPA scrutiny. CBP begins issuing detentions at a rate of 200 per day across the express carrier industry. Your cage facilities are at 110% capacity. Resolution requires supply chain traceability documentation that 80% of your importers do not have."

Each team responds. The consequences play out.

**Round 3: "The Platform Competitor"**

> "Amazon announces that its Supply Chain by Amazon service now includes fully automated customs clearance for third-party sellers — classification, duty calculation, compliance screening, and advance filing — at no additional cost. Sellers who use Supply Chain by Amazon see 99.2% straight-through processing. Amazon begins marketing this to the same e-commerce sellers who currently ship via FedEx."

Each team responds. The consequences play out.

**Hour 3: The Debrief (45 min)**

The facilitator brings all three teams together and leads a structured debrief:

1. **"Team Alpha: what was it like trying to respond without the platform?"** Let them describe the frustration. The manual workarounds. The impossibility of the speed required.

2. **"Team Beta: what was different?"** Let them describe the advantages. The automation. The speed. The competitive positioning.

3. **"Team Gamma: what surprised you?"** Let the DHL team describe what it felt like to be the competitor. What advantages did they have? Where were they vulnerable?

4. **"What does this tell us about 2026 — not 2028?"** The facilitator turns the conversation from the simulation to the real decision: the investment in the platform starts now or it doesn't.

The debrief should end with the operating leaders articulating the case themselves — not being told by a presenter. "We need to build this" is infinitely more powerful when it comes from the SVP of Clearance Operations than when it comes from a strategy deck.

### War Game Production Notes

- **The scenario cards should be physically printed and handed to each team.** Large format (11"×17"), with the scenario text, the key data, and the response questions. Physical objects create engagement that slides don't.
- **Each team needs a working space** — a table, a whiteboard or large paper pad, markers. They should be standing, not sitting. Standing groups make faster, more energetic decisions.
- **The facilitator needs a "consequences engine"** — a pre-prepared set of outcomes for each team's likely responses. These don't need to be computed. They need to be plausible. The facilitator announces them with authority.
- **Time pressure is essential.** 15 minutes per round is deliberately tight. It forces teams to prioritize, delegate, and make imperfect decisions — which is exactly what happens in real operations when a tariff shock hits.

### Who Facilitates

Not the same person who presents the demo. The war game facilitator should be someone the operating leaders respect as a peer — a senior operations leader, a former customs authority official, or a consulting partner with deep trade domain credibility. The facilitator is not selling the platform. They are creating the conditions for the participants to sell it to themselves.

## Experience 4: The Deep Dive

### What It Is

A 2-3 hour working session designed for the finance, strategy, and technology leaders who need to validate the business case before committing budget. This is not a presentation. It is a structured Q&A with supporting materials — a "data room" experience.

### Why It Exists

The Encounter creates curiosity. The Demo creates desire. The War Game creates urgency among operators. But none of these satisfy the CFO who needs to see the financial model, the CTO who needs to understand the technical architecture, or the strategy VP who needs to pressure-test the competitive positioning. The Deep Dive is for them.

### The Design

**Format:** Three stations, each staffed by a subject matter expert. Participants rotate through all three in small groups (3-5 people per group). Each station is 40-50 minutes. The stations run concurrently — this enables a 2.5-hour session for 15 people.

---

**Station 1: The Financial Model**

**Staffed by:** A finance-oriented team member who can build and defend a financial model live.

**Materials on display:**
- A projected spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Excel) showing the revenue model from the partnership vision, with editable assumptions.
- The market sizing data from the knowledge base (05_platform_partnership_research.md, Section 7).
- Comparable company valuations (WiseTech, Descartes, Global-e, Altana).

**How it works:** The finance expert walks through the model, then invites participants to challenge assumptions. "You think the FTA savings capture rate is too high? Let's change it. What's your number? OK, at 5% instead of 10%, the gain-share revenue drops to X. Still a [Y]-hundred-million-dollar line. What assumption do you want to test next?"

The model should be structured so that any single assumption can be changed without breaking the model, and the impact flows through immediately. This makes the session interactive and gives participants ownership of the numbers.

**Key financial model components:**

| Revenue Line | Assumptions to Test | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Automation cost savings | STP rate (40% → 98%), cost per manual entry, volume growth | Low sensitivity — the savings are large even at conservative STP targets |
| FTA gain-share | % of eligible trade unclaimed, capture rate, FedEx market share of FTA-eligible trade, gain-share % | Medium sensitivity — the unclaimed savings pool is large but the capture rate is uncertain |
| Landed cost API | Number of e-commerce platforms integrated, API call volume, price per call | High sensitivity — depends on platform adoption |
| Compliance subscription | Enterprise importer TAM, conversion rate, ARPU | Medium sensitivity |
| De minimis volume | Entry volume (known), automation rate, price per entry | Low sensitivity — the volume is enormous and growing |

**The "killer chart":** A single chart that should be on display throughout this station — a line graph showing FedEx's clearance cost per entry over time under two scenarios: (1) status quo (cost per entry rising as volume grows and complexity increases) and (2) with platform (cost per entry declining as automation increases, even as volume surges). The lines cross within 18-24 months. After the crossover, every additional entry on the platform generates margin, while every additional entry on the legacy system generates cost. This is the visual that CFOs understand instantly.

---

**Station 2: The Technical Architecture**

**Staffed by:** A senior technical architect (from Accenture or FedEx technology leadership) who can discuss platform design at the architectural level.

**Materials on display:**
- The platform architecture diagram from the partnership vision (the six engines + commerce layer + government filing layer).
- A federated data architecture diagram showing regional data nodes and the orchestration layer.
- An integration map showing connections to government systems (ACE, ICS2, CDS), commerce platforms (Shopify, SAP, etc.), and intelligence providers (Altana AI, etc.).

**How it works:** The architect walks through the technical approach, then fields questions. Key topics to be prepared for:

- **"How does the classification AI work?"** Answer: Multi-modal (NLP + vision + outcome learning). Trained on FedEx's clearance outcome data. Confidence scoring with human escalation. Not a single model — an ensemble approach with product-category-specific models. Initial deployment covers 90% of volume with the top 1,000 product categories.

- **"What's the data sovereignty approach?"** Answer: Federated regional data architecture. No single global database. Regional nodes in US, EU, China, APAC. Government filing data never leaves the jurisdiction. AI training uses privacy-preserving techniques (federated learning). The architecture is designed from day one for this — not retrofitted.

- **"How do you integrate with ACE and ICS2?"** Answer: Abstraction layer between the intelligence engines and the government filing interfaces. The engines produce classification, valuation, and compliance decisions. The filing layer translates those decisions into ACE, ICS2, CDS, or any other format. Swapping or upgrading a government interface does not require changing the intelligence layer. Accenture's experience building these government systems is the competitive advantage — they understand the interfaces from the inside.

- **"What about the legacy system transition?"** Answer: Phased migration by trade lane and customer segment. Not big-bang. Legacy systems and the new platform run in parallel during transition, with clear milestone-based retirement dates. The platform is designed to coexist with legacy systems, not replace them overnight.

- **"How long to build?"** Answer: [Do not give a time estimate per the system instructions. Instead:] Phase 1 delivers the first intelligence engines and launches on the top 10 trade lanes. Phase 2 scales to all major markets and all six engines. Phase 3 adds predictive capabilities and optimization. Phase 4 extends to platform APIs and external consumption. Each phase delivers value independently.

**The physical diagram:** Print the architecture diagram on a large poster (A1 or larger) and mount it on foam board. Having a physical diagram that participants can point at, annotate, and discuss around is more engaging than a projected slide. Provide sticky notes and markers so participants can flag questions or concerns directly on the diagram.

---

**Station 3: The Competitive Landscape and Risk Assessment**

**Staffed by:** A strategy or market intelligence expert who knows the competitive landscape cold.

**Materials on display:**
- The competitive landscape matrix from the knowledge base (03_competitive_landscape.md, Section 10).
- A "risk register" poster showing the key risks and mitigations from both vision documents.
- The TradeLens failure analysis — a concise one-page summary of why it failed and how this is different.

**How it works:** This station is designed to address skepticism head-on. The strategy expert says: "Your job in this station is to tell me why this won't work. I'll tell you how we've thought about it."

Anticipated challenges and designed responses:

| Challenge | Response |
|---|---|
| "DHL's TAS already does this." | TAS is a compliance tool for DHL's customers. It's good. But it's not a commerce platform — it doesn't embed clearance at the point of sale, doesn't offer landed cost APIs to e-commerce platforms, and doesn't have Accenture's government-side integration to optimize for next-gen customs systems. We're competing at a different altitude. |
| "Amazon will crush this." | Amazon's classification AI is better than ours for product catalog data. That's real. But Amazon serves Amazon's marketplace — not B2B, not industrial, not multi-carrier. And Amazon has better catalogs; we have better clearance outcomes. Clearance outcomes are what customs AI needs to learn from. |
| "This is TradeLens all over again." | TradeLens failed for three specific reasons: competitor governance (Maersk controlled it), solution in search of a problem (blockchain added complexity without value), and network-effect dependency (it needed everyone to join to be useful to anyone). Our platform avoids all three: FedEx controls it, it starts by solving FedEx's own clearance needs, and every participant gets individual value regardless of who else participates. |
| "The AI won't be accurate enough." | It won't be 100% accurate from day one. That's correct. The design includes confidence scoring with human escalation — low-confidence classifications go to expert brokers. The automation rate starts lower and grows. And the key metric isn't "is the AI perfect?" It's "is the AI better than the status quo?" The status quo has a 2-5% error rate on manual classification. We beat that on day one for high-confidence categories. |
| "Government systems won't modernize fast enough." | 80% of the value can be delivered on today's government infrastructure. Pre-clearance, advance filing, and automated classification work on current ACE and ICS2. The remaining 20% arrives as 21CCF, ACE 2.0, and the EU Customs Data Hub come online. We're designing for 2032 but deploying on 2024 systems. |

**The TradeLens poster:** A large, visible one-page comparison:

```
TradeLens (Failed)                    This Platform
─────────────────                     ─────────────
Controlled by competitor (Maersk)     Controlled by FedEx
Blockchain (complex, slow)            Cloud-native, API-first
Needed competitors to join            Serves FedEx's own operations first
Value required network effects        Each participant gets value alone
Revenue model never established       Gain-share tied to measurable value
```

This poster should be visible the entire time. It preempts the TradeLens comparison that skeptics will inevitably raise.

## Experience 5: The Retelling Kit

### What It Is

A set of physical and digital artifacts designed to help an internal champion sell the vision when you are not in the room. The executive who saw the demo needs to explain it to their CFO at dinner. The SVP who ran the war game needs to brief the CEO on Monday. The strategy VP needs to include it in a board presentation.

The Retelling Kit gives them the tools to do this without dilution.

### Why It Exists

Most big decisions are not made in the meeting where the pitch happens. They are made in the hallway afterward, in the follow-up email, in the board pre-read, in the conversation between the CFO and the CEO on a Friday afternoon. If the champion can't retell the story compellingly without you, the decision stalls.

### The Kit Contents

**1. The Executive Brief (10-page PDF)**

Designed in the companion demo document. A self-contained summary of the vision, the experience, the market opportunity, and the ask. Beautiful typography. Generous whitespace. One idea per page. Screenshots from the demo embedded.

**Structure:**

- Page 1: Title
- Page 2: The problem in four numbers
- Page 3: The shipper experience (Maria)
- Page 4: The importer experience (David's $480K savings)
- Page 5: The consumer experience (before/after)
- Page 6: The exception experience (7 days → 0 days)
- Page 7: The platform architecture (six engines)
- Page 8: The market opportunity ($2.2-12.3B)
- Page 9: The competitive window ("nobody has assembled the complete stack")
- Page 10: The ask

**2. The One-Pager**

A single page (front and back) that distills the entire vision into something that can be scanned in 60 seconds. This is the artifact that gets attached to an email with the subject line "Read this — I want to discuss Monday."

**Front:**

```
SEAMLESS CLEARANCE FOR GLOBAL COMMERCE

The Problem:
4M parcels/day now need formal US customs entry. Tariff rates exceed 100%
for some products. Compliance mandates (UFLPA, CBAM, EUDR) require data
no system was built to collect. And no platform — anywhere — solves the
whole problem.

The Platform:
Six intelligence engines — classification AI, duty calculation, compliance
screening, product intelligence, regulatory intelligence, predictive
exception resolution — that make customs clearance invisible.

The Experience:
• Shippers see landed cost at the point of sale. One click. No broker.
• Importers manage by exception, not by transaction. FTA savings found
  automatically. Compliance risk flagged before goods ship.
• Consumers see a single, all-inclusive price. No surprise duties.
  Delivery speed comparable to domestic.
• Exceptions resolve in hours, not days. Evidence pre-assembled by the
  platform.

The Opportunity:
$2.2-12.3B in annual revenue (Year 5), across automation savings, duty
optimization gain-share, API licensing, and premium clearance services.
```

**Back:**

```
WHY THIS, WHY NOW

• De minimis eliminated: millions of new entries per day, globally.
• Tariff stacking: rates > 100% on some products. Changed with days notice.
• UFLPA/CBAM/EUDR: new data demands no current system was designed for.
• Competitors investing: DHL (TAS), UPS (TradeAbility), Amazon (ML classification).
• Nobody has the complete stack: data + network + operations + government
  knowledge + platform engineering + AI capability.

THE ASK: [Specific investment amount, timeline, decision requested]
```

**3. The Tariff Card (from Experience 1)**

Include 5 of these in the kit. The champion can hand them to peers. A physical object that sits on a desk and starts conversations.

**4. The Demo Recording**

A screen recording of the Golden Path demo (Experiences 2), with the presenter's narration, edited to 12-15 minutes. Not a marketing video — the actual demo, recorded, with the real narration. This is the artifact the champion sends to someone who says "I want to see it but I wasn't in the room."

Production notes:
- Record during an actual rehearsal, not a special recording session. This keeps the energy natural.
- Edit only for length (cut pauses, remove fumbles) — do not add graphics, music, transitions, or production polish. The demo should look like a product being used, not a video being produced.
- Export at 1080p. Host on a private URL (not YouTube). The link should be shareable but not publicly discoverable.

**5. The Financial Model Summary**

A 2-page extract from the Deep Dive financial model. Shows the revenue lines, key assumptions, and the "killer chart" (cost per entry crossover). Include sensitivity analysis for the three most important assumptions. This is what the champion shows the CFO.

### Kit Packaging

- **Digital:** A private URL (something like a Notion page or a password-protected microsite) with the PDF brief, the one-pager, the demo recording, and the financial summary. Shareable by link.
- **Physical:** A custom folder or folio (FedEx purple, minimal branding) containing: the printed executive brief, 5 tariff cards, and the printed one-pager. Leave this with the champion after the demo.

The physical kit matters more than you think. A folder on a desk is a reminder. A PDF in a downloads folder is forgotten.

## Sequencing: The Conviction Journey

These five experiences are not interchangeable. They work in sequence.

```
Week 1-2:    THE ENCOUNTER
             ↓
             1-on-1 with 3-4 key executives
             Goal: Create curiosity. Get 60-minute meeting.
             ↓
Week 3-4:    THE GOLDEN PATH DEMO
             ↓
             10-30 decision-makers in a room
             Goal: Create desire. Make the future tangible.
             ↓
Week 5-6:    THE WAR GAME
             ↓
             12-20 operating leaders + their teams
             Goal: Create urgency. Show what happens if we don't act.
             ↓
Week 6-8:    THE DEEP DIVE
             ↓
             Finance, strategy, technology leaders
             Goal: Validate the business case. Answer every hard question.
             ↓
             THE RETELLING KIT
             ↓
             Distributed to all champions after each experience
             Goal: Enable internal advocacy when you're not in the room.
             ↓
Week 8-10:   THE DECISION
```

**Not every audience needs every experience.** Some paths:

- **If the CEO is already aligned:** Skip the Encounter. Go directly to the Demo with the CEO's endorsement. Then War Game for operators, Deep Dive for finance/tech.
- **If the primary audience is funders/investors (not internal executives):** Encounter → Demo → Deep Dive. Skip the War Game (it's designed for operators).
- **If the primary barrier is operator resistance (not executive buy-in):** War Game first. Let operators discover the problem themselves. Then Demo for executives, with operators as advocates.
- **If urgency is extreme (competitive threat is imminent):** Compress the timeline. Encounter and Demo in the same week. War Game the following week. Deep Dive concurrent. Decision in Week 4.

## What Makes This Strategy Work

### 1. Multiple formats for multiple brains.

Executives are not a monolith. Some are moved by stories (the Encounter). Some need to see the product (the Demo). Some need to feel the competitive pressure (the War Game). Some need to validate the numbers (the Deep Dive). Some need tools to champion internally (the Retelling Kit). The strategy covers all of them.

### 2. Each experience produces advocates, not just attendees.

The Encounter ends with the executive asking for more — they've taken the first step toward advocacy. The Demo ends with desire — they've seen the future and they want it. The War Game ends with the operators saying "we need this" — they've sold themselves. The Deep Dive ends with the skeptics saying "the numbers work" — they've validated the case. The Retelling Kit ends with champions spreading the vision — they're doing the work for you.

### 3. The physical artifacts are force multipliers.

The tariff card on a desk. The receipt in a pocket. The folio on a shelf. These are not decorations. They are conversation starters that extend the pitch into spaces and moments where you cannot be present. A physical object has a half-life of months. A PDF has a half-life of hours.

### 4. The War Game creates urgency without fear-mongering.

Telling executives "DHL will eat your lunch" is adversarial and can trigger defensiveness. Having their own operating leaders play through the scenario and discover the competitive threat themselves is education, not persuasion. The conclusion — "we need to move" — comes from inside, not outside.

### 5. The Retelling Kit acknowledges how decisions actually get made.

The decision-maker who sees the demo is not always the person who approves the budget. The person who approves the budget may never see the demo. The kit ensures that every intermediate conversation — the hallway pull-aside, the board pre-read, the Friday afternoon chat — has the right artifact available.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't lead with the technology.** Nobody funds "an AI platform for customs classification." They fund "the system that makes $480,000 in unclaimed savings appear on David's dashboard Monday morning." Lead with experience and outcome. Technology is the answer to "how" — not the pitch.

- **Don't demo to a cold room.** Pre-brief the 3-4 most influential people individually (the Encounter). When they see the demo with the full group, they're already believers. Their body language, their questions, their nods — these influence the rest of the room more than anything on the screen.

- **Don't show the vision documents.** The knowledge base and vision documents are the intellectual foundation. They are not the pitch. No executive reads a 690-line markdown document. The Encounter, the Demo, the War Game, the Deep Dive, and the Retelling Kit extract the right content for the right audience at the right moment. The documents stay in the background.

- **Don't let the perfect be the enemy of the deployed.** The demo will not be flawless. The financial model will have debatable assumptions. The war game facilitator will get challenged on a scenario. None of this matters if the overall experience creates conviction. A 90%-polished experience delivered this quarter beats a 100%-polished experience delivered next quarter — because the competitive window is closing.

- **Don't skip the ask.** Every experience ends with a specific next step. The Encounter: "Can I get an hour with your team?" The Demo: "Here's what we need to fund Phase 1." The War Game: "What's the decision timeline?" The Deep Dive: "What remaining questions would prevent a go-decision?" Vague endings produce vague outcomes.
