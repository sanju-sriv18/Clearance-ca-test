## 1. CBP Examination of Consolidated Cargo

### 1.1 CBP's Authority to Examine

Under **19 USC 1467**, U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) has the right to examine any shipment imported into the United States. The importer is required to bear the cost of those cargo exams. CBP uses intelligence from a number of sources to identify high-risk shipments and concentrates its inspectional resources accordingly.

### 1.2 How CBP Targets Specific HAWBs in Consolidations

CBP's targeting system applies a risk score to each shipment. When CBP issues an exam order on a specific HAWB (House Air Waybill) or HBL (House Bill of Lading) that is inside a ULD or container with other shipments, the following process occurs:

1. **Hold Notification**: CBP posts a hold code against the specific house bill (HAWB or HBL). Common hold codes include:
   - **1H**: Merchandise held per manually-posted order from CBP (general hold)
   - **7H**: Non-Intrusive Inspection (NII/VACIS) ordered
   - **5H**: Entry documents processing hold
   - **CET Hold (A-TCET)**: Anti-Terrorism Contraband Enforcement Team hold

2. **Master Bill Impact**: A hold on any HBOL (House Bill of Lading) results in the MBOL (Master Bill of Lading) remaining on hold. This means that if even ONE house bill within a consolidation has a hold, the entire master bill -- and thus the physical ULD or container -- remains on hold at the terminal level.

3. **Transfer for Examination**: The consolidation must be moved to a Centralized Examination Station (CES) or Container Freight Station (CFS) for deconsolidation before the targeted shipment can be physically separated and examined.

### 1.3 Does CBP Require Entire ULD/Container Breakdown?

**It depends on the exam type:**

- **VACIS/NII (X-Ray) Exam**: The entire ULD or container is scanned without breaking it down. The container seal is NOT broken. The unit is driven through or positioned near a large X-ray/gamma-ray machine. CBP reviews the images and either releases or escalates.

- **Tailgate Exam**: The ULD or container is opened and visually inspected. Officers break the seal and look inside without necessarily unloading all cargo. However, they may need to move some packages to access the targeted shipment.

- **Intensive Exam**: The ENTIRE ULD or container is moved to a CES facility, where it is fully unloaded (devanned/destuffed). All cargo is sorted, and CBP officers conduct thorough physical inspection of the targeted shipment(s). This DOES require complete breakdown of the unit.

### 1.4 CES (Centralized Examination Station) Procedures for Consolidated Cargo

A CES is a **privately operated facility**, not in the charge of a Customs officer, at which merchandise is made available to Customs officers for physical examination. CES operations are governed by **19 CFR Part 118** and **CBP Directive 3270-007A**.

**CES Procedures for Consolidated Cargo:**

1. **Notification**: The CES is notified of the exam hold via the Automated Manifest System (AMS). The notification specifies whether a tailgate or full strip exam is required.

2. **Transfer**: The container/ULD is transferred from the terminal to the CES under bond (using CBP Form 6043 delivery ticket). The transfer must occur under bond, with liability on the importer or agent per their importation and entry bond.

3. **Queue Processing**: All exams are conducted on a First-In, First-Out (FIFO) basis, EXCEPT for C-TPAT members who are prioritized and moved to the front.

4. **Examination**: The CES operator:
   - Breaks the seal on the container/ULD
   - Unloads (devans) the cargo
   - Separates and stages the targeted shipment for CBP inspection
   - Presents merchandise to CBP officers
   - CBP conducts the examination

5. **Release**: After examination, CBP releases the freight. CES notifies the broker or listed contact via email or other designated communication method.

6. **Reloading**: The CES facility reloads examined cargo and returns it for further transit. Neither CBP nor CES employees are responsible for damage during inspection. Cargo may NOT be repacked with the same care as originally.

**CES Operator Requirements:**
- Maintain facility per security standards in approved application
- Provide adequate personnel and equipment for opening, presenting, and closing all types of cargo
- Maintain U.S. Customs Service custodial bond
- Carry adequate liability insurance
- Provide office/parking space and facilities for customs officials
- Install intrusion detection systems
- Maintain physical barrier separating CES cargo from non-CBP cargo

### 1.5 VACIS/X-Ray Exam: ULD vs. Individual Packages

**VACIS (Vehicle and Cargo Inspection System)** uses gamma-ray technology to produce images of containers, ULDs, trucks, and other units:

- **On a ULD**: The entire ULD is scanned as a single unit. The system can process 150+ units per hour. Color-coded images distinguish organic materials (explosives, drugs) from inorganic materials (guns, shielded objects). The ULD does NOT need to be opened.

- **On Individual Packages**: If the VACIS scan of the ULD reveals anomalies, CBP may escalate to tailgate or intensive exam. At that point, individual packages within the ULD may be scanned separately or physically inspected.

- **Key Limitation**: While VACIS can detect anomalies in a ULD, it cannot precisely identify which specific HAWB/package is problematic within a densely packed consolidation. This is why escalation to physical exam (and therefore ULD breakdown) is common.

**VACIS Technical Capabilities:**
- 6/4 MeV linear accelerator X-ray source with dual-energy material discrimination
- Stationary portal design
- Can scan automatically as units pass through
- Deployable in approximately 10 minutes
- Operates on its own power generator or standard 220 VAC shore power

### 1.6 Non-Targeted Shipments in the Same Consolidation

**What happens to non-targeted shipments:**

- **During VACIS/NII Exam**: All shipments remain in the ULD/container. No individual shipment is separately affected -- the entire unit is scanned and released (or escalated) together.

- **During Intensive Exam**: All shipments in the consolidation are physically affected because the ULD/container must be fully broken down. However:
  - Notices of Detention are **product-specific, not shipment-specific**
  - Non-targeted shipments CAN be released after deconsolidation, provided they have their own CBP-authorized movement
  - The CFS/CES can "release cargo that has been cleared through CBP/relevant agencies and withhold cargo that is not released/under hold"

- **Cost Impact**: When shipping consolidated (LCL), exam fees are typically split between all importers sharing the container/ULD, regardless of who was flagged. The freight forwarder calculates and collects the proportional fees. For VACIS exams specifically, the cost is spread over the entire container.

- **Delay Impact**: ALL shipments in the consolidation will be delayed, even non-targeted ones, because the physical unit cannot be released until either (a) the exam is complete, or (b) the unit is broken down at a CFS/CES and individual cleared shipments are separated.

## 2. Consolidation Paperwork and Manifests

### 2.1 ULD Manifest

A **ULD manifest** is a document that itemizes the contents of a single Unit Load Device. Each ULD has its own packing list/manifest so that its contents can be tracked, which speeds up tracking and customs processing since each ULD travels under one ID number and manifest.

**A ULD manifest contains:**
- ULD identification number (3 leading letters + 4-5 numerals + 2 trailing letters per IATA standard)
- All HAWB numbers loaded in the ULD
- For each HAWB:
  - Full shipper name and address
  - Consignee name and address
  - Number of pieces
  - Weight (kg/lb)
  - Complete commodity description
- Consolidator identification
- De-consolidator identification
- Total piece count and weight for the ULD
- SLAC (Shipper's Load and Count) -- the actual number of pieces within or on each loaded unit

**Importance**: When a ULD contains a consolidation, the forwarder MUST deliver an (electronic) ULD manifest with exact HAWB details for each individual house waybill. This prevents the need to open document pouches to determine HAWB information.

### 2.2 CBP Air Cargo Manifest (Customs Form 7509)

The official U.S. customs air cargo manifest form contains:
- Owner/Operator information
- Marks of Nationality and Registration
- Flight Number
- Port of Lading and Port of Unlading
- Date
- **Consolidator** (used for consolidation shipments only)
- **De-Consolidator** (used for consolidation shipments only)
- For each air waybill:
  - Consignee Name and Address
  - Nature of Goods
  - Shipper Name and Address
  - Air Waybill Number (11-digit)
  - Air Waybill Type: **M** = Master, **H** = House, **S** = Sub
  - Number of Pieces
  - Weight (Kg/Lb)
  - Number of HAWBs (for consolidated shipments)

When copies of air waybills are attached, the statement "Cargo as per air waybills attached" must appear on the manifest.

### 2.3 Ocean Container Manifest / Packing List for Consolidated Containers

For ocean LCL (Less-than-Container Load) shipments:

**Container Manifest** lists:
- Container number and seal number
- All shipments consolidated within the container
- For each shipment: shipper, consignee, HBL number, cargo description, volume (CBM), and weight

**Packing List** details:
- Contents, dimensions, weights, and markings of each individual shipment
- Package-level detail within each HBL

The container travels under a **Master Bill of Lading (MBL)** issued by the shipping line to the freight forwarder/NVOCC. Each individual shipment has its own **House Bill of Lading (HBL)** issued by the forwarder to the shipper.

### 2.4 Consolidation Manifest in Freight Forwarding

A **consolidation manifest** is the document that ties the master document to all individual house documents within a consolidated shipment.

**Hierarchy:**

| Level | Air Freight | Ocean Freight | Issued By | Issued To |
|-------|-------------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Carrier** | Master Air Waybill (MAWB) | Master Bill of Lading (MBL) | Airline / Shipping Line | Freight Forwarder/NVOCC |
| **House** | House Air Waybill (HAWB) | House Bill of Lading (HBL) | Freight Forwarder | Individual Shipper |
| **Cargo Manifest** | Air Cargo Manifest (CF 7509) | Vessel Cargo Declaration | Carrier | Customs Authorities |
| **Consolidation Manifest** | ULD Manifest / Consolidation Manifest | Container Packing List | Forwarder | Carrier, Customs |

**Key relationships:**
- One MAWB covers the entire consolidation; multiple HAWBs are grouped under it
- One MBL covers the entire container; multiple HBLs are grouped under it
- The consolidation manifest maps which HAWBs/HBLs are in which ULD/container
- The cargo manifest (submitted to customs) references the master document and can attach house documents

### 2.5 How Manifests Relate to Customs Entry Documents

- **Master level entry**: For consolidated shipments, the word "Consolidation" is a sufficient cargo description for the master air waybill/bill of lading record. The consignee can be identified as the container station, express consignment operator, or other carrier.

- **House level entry**: Each individual HAWB/HBL requires a separate customs entry. U.S. Customs (CBP) now allows authorized cargo releases at the House Bill of Lading (HBL) level, not just the Master Bill level, providing greater flexibility for freight forwarders managing consolidated shipments.

- **Separate entry procedures**: Under 19 CFR 141.54, each portion of a consolidated shipment addressed to one nominal consignee for various ultimate consignees may be entered separately. The nominal consignee must waive the right to have the bill of lading/air waybill returned and provide certificates for each portion.

### 3.2 CBP Directives on ULD and Container Examination

- **CBP Directive 3270-007A** (May 6, 2012): Primary directive governing Centralized Examination Stations. Covers CES application, operation agreements, security standards, staffing requirements, and billing procedures.

- **Containerized Cargo Sealing Policy**: CBP officers affix ISO/PAS 17712 compliant high-security bolt seals upon conclusion of inspection, marked with "CBP" and a unique alphanumeric serial number.

- **National Maritime Targeting Policy** (CBP Directive 3290-007B, December 28, 2007): Maritime targeting procedures.

- **Non-Intrusive Inspection Directive**: Governs use of VACIS and other NII technology at ports.

- **CBP Cargo Description Requirements** (2024-2025): CBP now rejects vague cargo descriptions in ACAS filings. "Consolidated" is ONLY acceptable at the master bill level. All house-level descriptions must be specific (e.g., "gift," "daily necessities," "accessories," "parts" are now rejected). A precise cargo description or 6-digit HTS number is required. Effective November 12, 2024.

### 3.3 TSA Requirements for ULD Manifests and Air Cargo Security

**TSA Mandates:**
- **100% Screening Mandate**: Per the 9/11 Commission Act of 2007, TSA requires 100% of cargo on passenger aircraft to be screened at a level commensurate with checked baggage screening.

- **Manifest Accuracy**: Procedures must ensure that the information in the carrier's cargo manifest accurately reflects the information provided by the shipper. Cargo must be properly marked and manifested including accurate weight and piece count.

- **ULD/Container Inspection**: For cargo containers or ULDs, the air carrier must work with business partners to ensure pertinent security measures are in place. Detailed physical inspection of the customer's cargo container/ULD is required prior to loading onto the aircraft.

- **Approved Screening Methods**: TSA-approved x-ray systems, explosives detection systems (EDS), explosives trace detection (ETD), TSA-certified canine teams, physical search together with manifest verification.

- **Certified Cargo Screening Program (CCSP)**: TSA certifies cargo screening facilities throughout the U.S. to screen cargo BEFORE providing it to airlines. Cargo accepted from a Certified Cargo Screening Facility (CCSF) does NOT require additional screening.

- **Known Shipper Program**: TSA identifies and approves "known shipper" status for qualified shippers. Known shipper cargo is separated from unknown shipper cargo. This is an information-based screening method.

- **Chain of Custody**: Carrier must verify chain of custody for screened cargo is intact prior to loading, or re-screen.

- **Air Cargo Advance Screening (ACAS)**: Joint CBP/TSA program. Carriers submit manifest data to CBP's Automated Targeting System (ATS) prior to loading at all foreign last-point-of-departure airports. ATS automatically performs risk assessment for CBRNE threats.

### 3.4 IATA Regulations for ULD Content Documentation

**IATA ULD Regulations (ULDR)** -- the comprehensive reference manual covering:

- **ULD Classification and Identification**: Standard identification system (3 letters + 4-5 numerals + 2 letters). Every ULD must have a manufacturer's plate/marking (TSO plate) that is attached and legible.

- **ULD Content Documentation Requirements**:
  - Forwarder must deliver electronic ULD manifest with exact HAWB details
  - SLAC (Shipper's Load and Count) must be recorded
  - Content verification requirements since receiving airline cannot physically verify what has been loaded

- **IATA Resolution 606 (Bar Coded Label)**: Standardizes labeling for air freight. Labels contain:
  - Airway Bill barcode (airline number + master AWB number + running number)
  - Total number of pieces
  - House Air Waybill number
  - Areas for airline and company official use

- **IATA Cargo Services Conference Resolution Manual (CSCRM)**: Covers cargo handling, documentation, procedures, ULD control, ULD technical specifications, and automation standards.

- **Dangerous Goods in ULDs**: Maximum net quantity per package shown in DGR List. Must comply with applicable packing instructions. Certain dangerous goods may not be included in any freight container or ULD unless exempted under DGR.

### 3.5 WCO Framework for Consolidated Shipment Inspection

The **World Customs Organization (WCO)** provides the global framework through:

- **SAFE Framework of Standards** (adopted 2005, updated 2021): Non-binding instrument with 5 core elements:
  1. Harmonized advance electronic cargo information for inbound, outbound, and transit shipments
  2. Consistent risk management approach to address security threats
  3. Use of non-intrusive inspection (NII) technology for high-risk shipments
  4. Benefits to businesses meeting minimum supply chain security standards (AEO concept)
  5. Facilitation of seamless movement through secure supply chains

- **NII Guidelines** (updated June 2025): Includes Unified File Format (UFF) for NII data. Enables centralized image interpretation across scanner brands. Scanners can be networked and images sent to central server.

- **Authorized Economic Operator (AEO) Program**: AEOs may include manufacturers, importers, exporters, brokers, carriers, **consolidators**, intermediaries, ports, airports, terminal operators, integrated operators, warehouses, distributors, and freight forwarders. AEO status provides reduced examination rates.

- **E-Commerce Framework of Standards** (2018): Addresses risk management and facilitation of growing B2C and C2C cross-border small/low-value shipments.

## 4. Operational Impact of Customs Holds on Consolidations

### 4.1 When One Handling Unit Is Held -- Can the Rest Be Released?

**Short answer: Yes, eventually, but it requires physical deconsolidation first.**

**Detailed process:**

1. **At Terminal Level**: A hold on ANY house bill causes the ENTIRE master bill to remain on hold. The terminal cannot release the physical container/ULD for any shipment.

2. **Permit to Transfer (PTT)**: To break the logjam, a 1W (Permit to Transfer) can be issued at the master bill level, allowing the FULL container/ULD to be moved to a CFS/CES for deconsolidation, even while individual house bills remain on hold.

3. **1Z Authorization**: Once all house bills have a CBP-authorized movement (which can include a 1W for held shipments), the master bill receives a 1Z, allowing physical release from the terminal.

4. **At the CFS/CES**: After physical deconsolidation, the CFS/CES CAN release individual shipments that have been cleared by CBP while withholding held shipments.

5. **1X Transfer for Exam**: This code allows cargo to move to an exam facility while there is still an active hold on other shipments.

**Key CBP Authorization Codes:**
| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **1Z** | Master bill may be physically released (all house bills have authorized movement) |
| **1W** | Permit to Transfer to CFS/CES for deconsolidation |
| **1X** | Transfer for Exam |
| **1H** | Manual CBP hold |
| **7H** | NII (VACIS) exam ordered |
| **1I** | Hold removed |
| **1B/4C** | Entry release |

### 4.2 Break-Bulk Procedures at CES for Consolidated Cargo

The CES break-bulk process for consolidated cargo follows these steps:

1. **Container/ULD received at CES** under bonded transfer
2. **Seal verified and broken** in controlled environment
3. **Full unload (devan/destuff)**: All cargo removed from container/ULD
4. **Sorting**: Packages separated by HAWB/HBL and staged
5. **Examination**: Targeted shipments presented to CBP officers
6. **Sampling/Testing**: CBP may take samples; if needed, sent to Customs-accredited laboratories
7. **Documentation**: CES maintains records; CBP Form 6043 tracks movements
8. **Release**: Cleared shipments released; held shipments remain in CES custody
9. **Reload**: Released cargo may be reloaded or forwarded separately

### 4.3 Time and Cost Implications

**Time Implications by Exam Type:**

| Exam Type | Typical Duration | Impact on Consolidation |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **VACIS/NII** | 1-3 days | Minimal -- entire unit scanned, no breakdown needed |
| **Tailgate** | 2-5 days (ocean); hours (air) | Moderate -- container opened but may not be fully unloaded |
| **Intensive** | 7-30 days | Severe -- full breakdown, all shipments delayed unless deconsolidated |

**Cost Components for Consolidated Cargo Exam:**

| Cost Item | Approximate Range | Who Pays |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| **VACIS/NII scan** | $150-$300 per container/ULD | Split among all importers in consolidation |
| **Drayage to CES** | Varies by distance | Importer(s) |
| **CES devanning/destuffing** | $350-$2,500+ | Split among importers or charged to targeted importer |
| **CES storage** | Per-day charges after free period | Importer |
| **Demurrage (terminal)** | $75-$300/day per container | Importer |
| **Per diem (ULD/container equipment)** | Per-day charges after free days | Importer |
| **Reloading** | Varies | Importer |
| **Trucking delays (dry runs)** | Varies | Importer |
| **Chassis use** | Per-day charge | Importer |

**Escalating Demurrage Example:**
- Days 1-3 (beyond free time): ~$100/day
- Days 4-9: ~$250/day
- Days 10+: ~$350/day
- A 10-container shipment held 14 days (with 3 free days) = approximately $25,000 in demurrage alone

### 4.4 Who Bears the Cost of Deconsolidation for Customs Exam?

**Legal framework**: Under **19 CFR 151.6**, the importer shall bear any expense involved in preparing the merchandise for CBP examination and in the closing of packages. No distinction is made between commercial and personal shipments.

**In practice:**
- For **LCL/consolidated shipments**, the costs are typically divided proportionally between the importers sharing the container/ULD, based on volume or number of packages
- The **freight forwarder** (arrival agent) calculates and collects the proportional fees
- **CBP does NOT charge for the examination itself** -- but all associated logistics costs (transport to CES, devanning, storage, reloading) fall on the importer(s)
- **CBP does not disclose which specific shipper triggered the exam** and splits costs among all cargo owners for security purposes
- The **CES operator** bills the Beneficiary Cargo Owner or their legal representative

### 4.5 How Express Carriers Handle This Differently

Express carriers (FedEx, UPS, DHL) operate fundamentally differently from traditional freight forwarders when it comes to customs examination of consolidated cargo:

**Express Consignment Carrier Facilities (ECCFs):**
- Governed by **19 CFR Part 128**
- ECCFs are specialized bonded warehouse facilities approved by CBP specifically for examination and release of express consignment shipments
- Major express carriers (FedEx, UPS, DHL) operate their own ECCFs at major airports (Memphis, Louisville/Worldport, Cincinnati)
- CBP officers are stationed ON-SITE at these facilities

**Key Differences:**

| Aspect | Express Carriers (ECCF) | Freight Forwarders (CES/CFS) |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Facility** | Carrier-operated ECCF at hub airports | Third-party CES or CFS facilities |
| **CBP Presence** | On-site, often 24/7 at major hubs | Scheduled visits or by appointment |
| **Clearance Speed** | >90% same-day clearance (FedEx EXPRESSCLEAR) | 2-30 days depending on exam type |
| **Package Handling** | Individual package scanning and sorting as part of normal operations | Requires special deconsolidation effort |
| **Cost Structure** | Included in shipping fees or charged as ancillary service | Separate CES/CFS fees, drayage, storage |
| **Section 321 Processing** | Most parcels <$800 cleared via Section 321 on consolidated eManifest | Less common; most shipments require formal/informal entry |
| **Technology** | Fully integrated with ACE, ABI, AMS | Varies by CFS/CES operator |
| **Hub Model** | All packages flow through central hub anyway; exam is part of normal sort | Exam requires diversion from normal routing |

**Express carrier advantages:**
- Packages are already individually handled and sorted as part of normal hub operations (at UPS Worldport, human hands touch each package only 3 times)
- Pulling one package for exam doesn't require breaking down an entire ULD because the sort process already does this
- Processing fees prescribed in 19 CFR 24.23 cover examination costs
- Each express carrier must sign a narcotics enforcement agreement with ICE
- Express carriers maintain automated systems (AMS, ABI, Cargo Selectivity) for real-time processing

## 5. Express Carriers vs. Freight Forwarders

### 5.1 Section 321 De Minimis and Express Consolidations

Most parcel shipments entering ECCFs fall under **Section 321** ($800 or less), submitted under consolidated air manifests using the ACE Air eManifest system. Section 321 shipments:
- Must be separated on the manifest from >$800 shipments
- Do not need HTSUS subheading number or entry summary when using eManifest
- Are duty- and tax-free

**Note**: Following the 2025 elimination of de minimis for certain countries, express carriers have adapted their processes. UPS, FedEx, and DHL all report different approaches to handling packages that fail to clear customs under the new rules.

### 5.2 Express Carrier Consolidation Model

Express carriers consolidate at the ULD level differently than freight forwarders:
- Packages are consolidated by **destination** not by **shipper**
- Each package maintains individual tracking and manifest data
- The hub sort process naturally deconsolidates during operations
- Customs targeting can pull individual packages without affecting the broader consolidation

## 6. Industry Terminology

### 6.1 "Break Bulk" in Customs Examination Context

The term "break bulk" has multiple meanings depending on context:

1. **Traditional shipping meaning**: Cargo that is too large for standard containers and must be loaded/unloaded as individual pieces (e.g., heavy machinery, vehicles, structural steel). This is non-containerized, non-unitized cargo.

2. **Customs examination meaning**: The process of opening/breaking open consolidated cargo for inspection. An "intensive exam" is sometimes called a "break bulk exam" because the container/ULD must be completely broken down and each package inspected individually.

3. **Express carrier meaning (DHL BBX)**: DHL's "Breakbulk" (BBX) service specifically refers to a consolidated shipment cleared through customs as one entry, then broken down into individual "baby" shipments for last-mile delivery.

4. **General logistics meaning**: The process of reversing a consolidation at destination -- converting cargo from a consolidated/unitized condition back to individual packages.

### 6.2 Exam Types for Consolidated Cargo

**Tailgate Exam:**
- Container/ULD seal is broken, doors opened
- Visual inspection by CBP officers -- they look but do not touch/unload cargo
- If nothing suspicious is seen, the unit is resealed and released
- Duration: 2-5 days (ocean), 1-few hours (air)
- Cost: ~$350 plus transportation
- Impact on consolidation: Moderate -- all shipments are briefly delayed but the unit is not fully broken down

**Intensive Exam:**
- Full unload at CES facility
- All cargo removed, sorted, staged for CBP inspection
- Individual boxes may be opened, samples taken
- Duration: 7-30 days
- Cost: $1,000-$2,500+
- Impact on consolidation: Severe -- all shipments fully delayed; physical separation required
- CBP and CES are NOT responsible for any damage
- Cargo will NOT be repacked with same care as originally

**VACIS/NII Exam:**
- Non-intrusive X-ray/gamma-ray scan
- Container seal is NOT broken
- Duration: 1-3 days
- Cost: $150-$300
- Impact on consolidation: Minimal -- entire unit scanned as one, no breakdown
- If anomaly detected, may escalate to tailgate or intensive

### 6.3 "General Order" for Consolidated Cargo

Under **19 CFR Part 127**, merchandise goes into "General Order" (GO) when entry is not made within the prescribed time (15 calendar days after landing from vessel, aircraft, or vehicle).

**For consolidated cargo:**
- If a consolidation is not entered/claimed within 15 days, the ENTIRE consolidation may be placed in General Order
- GO merchandise is deposited in public stores or a General Order warehouse at the risk and expense of the consignee
- After 6 months in GO, merchandise is considered unclaimed and abandoned
- Title vests in the U.S. government (free and clear of liens) 30 days after notice to known interested parties
- The carrier is liable for liquidated damages under their bond if they refuse to relinquish custody to a GO warehouse

**Special issues with consolidated GO cargo:**
- Individual HAWBs/HBLs within a consolidation may have different entry deadlines or clearance status
- A single unclaimed shipment can complicate the entire consolidation
- Claims for surplus proceeds from sale of GO merchandise must be supported by the original bill of lading

### 6.4 "Devanning" and "Destuffing" in Customs Context

**Devanning** (also called container unloading, container stripping, or destuffing): The process of removing cargo from a shipping container or ULD. In the customs context:

- **At CES**: Devanning is the critical first step in an intensive customs exam. The CES operator devans the container/ULD to separate and present cargo to CBP officers.
- **At CFS**: Devanning is part of the normal deconsolidation process for LCL shipments. Customs seals are typically removed in the presence of a customs officer at the time of destuffing.
- **FCL vs. LCL**: FCL shipments are typically devanned at the destination warehouse; LCL shipments are devanned at the destination CFS.

**Destuffing** is synonymous with devanning but more commonly used in ocean freight contexts. The process includes:
1. Verify container condition and seal integrity
2. Break seal (in presence of customs officer if required)
3. Open doors carefully (watch for cargo shifts during transit)
4. Visual inspection
5. Physical unloading (manual, forklift, or specialized equipment)
6. Check against packing list; note discrepancies
7. Sort and stage for delivery or further customs processing

**Deconsolidation** is a broader process than devanning/destuffing:
- Includes devanning as the first step
- Also includes sorting shipments by consignee/destination
- Administrative tasks (documentation verification, customs processing)
- Preparation for final delivery
- Primarily applies to LCL/consolidated shipments

## 7. ACAS (Air Cargo Advance Screening) and Consolidation Targeting

### 7.1 ACAS Program Structure

The ACAS program requires submission of advance cargo data BEFORE loading onto aircraft destined to or transiting through the United States.

**Mandatory ACAS Data Elements (per HAWB for consolidated shipments):**
1. Air waybill number
2. Total quantity based on smallest external packing unit
3. Total weight of cargo
4. Cargo description (MUST be specific; generic terms rejected since Nov 2024)
5. Shipper name and address (MUST be actual foreign manufacturer/supplier -- NOT the consolidator or forwarder)
6. Consignee name and address (actual delivery party)

**For consolidated shipments:**
- MAWB-level filing by the inbound air carrier
- HAWB-level filing by either the carrier or the freight forwarder
- Freight forwarders are specifically included as eligible filers because they typically have HAWB data earlier than the carrier

### 7.2 Enhanced ACAS (2024-2025)

Following security incidents in July 2024, CBP and TSA enhanced the ACAS program:

- ACAS Implementation Guide updated to version 2.3.1 (August 30, 2024)
- New data elements for more complete picture of parties in cargo transactions
- Enhanced relationship data between parties
- Financial data related to cargo shipments
- Vague cargo description rejections mandatory from November 12, 2024
- Enhanced ACAS rulemaking published November 2025 (Federal Register)

### 7.3 "Do Not Load" (DNL) Actions

When ACAS targeting identifies high-risk cargo:
- CBP can issue "Do Not Load" orders BEFORE cargo is loaded onto aircraft
- This affects individual HAWBs, not necessarily the entire consolidation
- The earlier the submission, the sooner CBP can communicate DNL or approval
- This is a major advantage of HAWB-level filing -- targeted intervention without disrupting entire consolidation
