# Global Customs Clearance: Regulatory Landscape

> **FedEx Global Clearance Knowledge Base**
> Last Updated: January 2026

---

## Executive Summary

The global regulatory environment for customs clearance is undergoing the most significant transformation in decades. The convergence of de minimis elimination, escalating tariff regimes, new compliance mandates (UFLPA, CBAM, EUDR), and sweeping customs modernization legislation is fundamentally reshaping how goods cross borders. For FedEx, understanding this landscape is not optional — it is the foundation upon which our clearance strategy must be built.

---

## 1. United States: De Minimis Elimination

### Section 321 Suspension (Effective August 29, 2025)

The United States has suspended the de minimis exemption under Section 321 of the Tariff Act for **all countries of origin**. This is the single most disruptive regulatory change to express clearance operations in a generation.

**What Changed:**
- Previously, shipments valued at $800 or less entered the US duty-free and with minimal data requirements under Section 321 (also known as informal entry or "de minimis" entry).
- As of August 29, 2025, **all imports regardless of value** require formal entry with full customs data, including:
  - 10-digit HTSUS classification
  - Country of origin
  - Manufacturer/supplier identification
  - Full commercial invoice with accurate valuation
  - Applicable PGA data (FDA, CPSC, EPA, etc.)

**Scale of Impact:**
- Approximately **4 million parcels per day** previously entered the US under Section 321.
- These parcels now require formal entry processing — equivalent to adding millions of new customs entries daily to a system already operating near capacity.
- The express carrier industry (FedEx, UPS, DHL) bears the operational brunt, as the majority of de minimis shipments move via express and postal channels.

**Regulatory Background:**
- The STOP De Minimis Imports from Foreign Adversaries Act and the De Minimis Reciprocity Act both advanced through Congress in 2024-2025.
- Executive action under IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act) and Section 301 authorities also targeted de minimis, particularly for China-origin goods.
- CBP issued interim final rules requiring advance electronic data for all shipments, regardless of value.
- Entry Type 86 (a simplified entry designed for low-value e-commerce with Section 321 benefits) is effectively deprecated.

**Operational Implications for FedEx:**
- Massive increase in formal entry volume requiring classification, valuation, and PGA screening.
- Shipper data quality becomes critical — many e-commerce shippers have never provided HTSUS codes or detailed product descriptions.
- Caging and exam capacity must scale to handle the volume of holds on newly formalized parcels.
- Technology systems (ACE filings, broker workstations) must handle throughput orders of magnitude higher than historical formal entry volumes.

## 2. United States: Tariff Complexity and Escalation

### The Layered Tariff Regime

US tariff policy has become extraordinarily complex, with multiple tariff programs stacking on top of the standard Most Favored Nation (MFN) duty rates. Understanding the layering is essential for accurate duty calculation and clearance.

**Section 301 Tariffs (Trade Act of 1974):**
- Originally imposed on approximately $370 billion of Chinese-origin goods in four tranches (Lists 1–4) beginning in 2018.
- Rates range from 7.5% to 25% on top of MFN duties.
- In 2024-2025, Section 301 tariffs were **expanded and increased** following the USTR's four-year review:
  - Electric vehicles from China: 100% tariff (effective 2024)
  - Semiconductors: 50% (effective 2025)
  - Solar cells: 50% (effective 2024)
  - Steel and aluminum: 25% (maintained)
  - Lithium-ion batteries: 25% (effective 2024-2026, phased)
  - Ship-to-shore cranes: 25% (effective 2024)
  - Medical gloves, syringes, PPE: 25-50% (effective 2024-2025)
  - Critical minerals: Various rates effective 2024-2026

**Section 232 Tariffs (Trade Expansion Act of 1962):**
- 25% tariff on steel imports and 10% tariff on aluminum imports from most countries.
- Applies based on **country of melt and pour** (steel) or **country of smelt and cast** (aluminum), not just country of export.
- Product-specific exclusions exist but require separate petitions and have limited duration.
- In 2025, Section 232 tariffs were extended to **derivative products** (downstream steel and aluminum products).

**IEEPA Reciprocal Tariffs (International Emergency Economic Powers Act):**
- Beginning in early 2025, the administration invoked IEEPA authority to impose "reciprocal" tariffs designed to mirror the tariff and non-tariff barriers that other countries impose on US goods.
- These are **additive** to existing tariff programs.
- Rates vary by country but have reached:
  - China: 60-145% (combined with Section 301 and other programs, effective rates exceed 100% on many goods)
  - EU: 20% baseline reciprocal tariff
  - Other countries: 10-50% depending on bilateral trade balance assessment
- IEEPA tariffs have been subject to rapid changes, pauses, and modifications, creating significant compliance uncertainty.

**Effective Tariff Rate Stacking Example (China-origin consumer electronics):**
| Tariff Layer | Rate |
|---|---|
| MFN Duty | 0-6% |
| Section 301 (List 4a) | 7.5% |
| IEEPA Reciprocal | 60% |
| **Total Effective Rate** | **67.5-73.5%** |

For some product categories (steel, EVs, batteries), combined rates exceed **100%** of declared value.

**Anti-Dumping and Countervailing Duties (AD/CVD):**
- Hundreds of active AD/CVD orders covering products from dozens of countries.
- Rates can exceed 200% for specific manufacturers/exporters (e.g., Chinese solar panels, Vietnamese steel).
- AD/CVD rates are company-specific and change annually based on administrative reviews.
- Evasion enforcement has intensified, with CBP's Trade Remedy Law Enforcement Directorate (TRLED) and the EAPA (Enforce and Protect Act) investigations.

### Classification Complexity

- The Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States (HTSUS) contains **over 17,000 unique tariff lines** at the 10-digit level.
- With tariff programs layering, the same physical product can have dramatically different duty rates depending on:
  - Country of origin (and whether substantial transformation occurred)
  - Which tariff programs apply (Section 301, 232, IEEPA, AD/CVD)
  - Whether exclusions or exemptions were granted
  - Applicable free trade agreements (USMCA, KORUS, etc.)
- Classification rulings from CBP can take **months** and are sometimes contradictory.
- Misclassification risk has increased substantially — the financial exposure from incorrect classification has never been higher given the stacking of penalty tariff programs.

## 3. United States: UFLPA and Forced Labor Enforcement

### Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA)

Enacted in December 2021 with full enforcement beginning June 2022, UFLPA has become one of the most operationally disruptive compliance requirements in US customs.

**Core Mechanism:**
- Creates a **rebuttable presumption** that all goods mined, produced, or manufactured in whole or in part in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region (XUAR) of China are made with forced labor and are **prohibited** from entry into the United States.
- The presumption extends to goods produced by entities on the **UFLPA Entity List** maintained by the Forced Labor Enforcement Task Force (FLETF), regardless of where those entities operate.
- Importers must provide **clear and convincing evidence** that goods were not produced with forced labor to overcome the presumption — one of the highest evidentiary standards in US trade law.

**Enforcement Scale:**
- CBP has detained and/or denied entry to shipments valued at over $3 billion since enforcement began.
- Priority sectors: Cotton and cotton products, polysilicon and solar products, tomatoes and tomato-based products, seafood, PVC/plastics.
- In 2024-2025, enforcement expanded to **downstream products** — goods that contain components or raw materials from the XUAR, even if the finished product was manufactured elsewhere.

**Supply Chain Tracing Requirements:**
- To overcome a UFLPA detention, importers must demonstrate full supply chain traceability from raw material to finished product.
- Required evidence includes: supplier lists, audit reports, bills of material, shipping records, payment records, and third-party verification.
- This has created an entirely new compliance discipline — **supply chain mapping** — that many importers lack the systems and data to perform.

**Impact on Express Clearance:**
- UFLPA detentions can occur on any shipment flagged by CBP's targeting algorithms.
- Detained shipments are placed in **general order** and, if not resolved within timelines, may be subject to seizure, export, or destruction.
- E-commerce shipments (particularly apparel, textiles, electronics) are at high risk.
- Resolution of a UFLPA detention typically takes **weeks to months**, creating significant cage dwell time and importer frustration.

## 4. European Union: Customs Reform Package

### The Most Ambitious EU Customs Overhaul in Decades

The European Commission adopted the EU Customs Reform Package in May 2023, proposing the most comprehensive modernization of EU customs since the Union Customs Code (UCC) entered into force in 2016. The reform is currently progressing through trilogue negotiations between the European Parliament and Council.

### Key Components

**Elimination of the EUR 150 Duty Exemption:**
- Currently, goods valued at EUR 150 or less entering the EU are exempt from customs duties (though VAT applies via the Import One-Stop Shop / IOSS mechanism introduced in July 2021).
- Under the reform, **customs duties will apply to all goods regardless of value**, effective July 2026.
- A simplified duty structure is proposed: a **flat rate of EUR 3 per item** for goods ordered via e-commerce platforms, replacing the need for item-by-item classification.
- E-commerce platforms (marketplaces) will become the **deemed importer** and will be responsible for collecting and remitting duties and taxes.

**EU Customs Data Hub (Target: 2028-2032):**
- A centralized EU-wide data platform replacing the current patchwork of 27 national customs IT systems.
- All customs data — import, export, transit — will flow through the Data Hub.
- The Data Hub will use **AI and advanced analytics** for risk assessment, automated classification, and fraud detection.
- Traders will submit data once to the Hub; it will be shared with all relevant authorities across member states.
- Phased implementation: initial capabilities by 2028, full deployment by 2032.

**Trust and Check Traders:**
- An enhanced version of the Authorized Economic Operator (AEO) program.
- "Trust and Check" traders will be permitted to:
  - Release goods into free circulation **before completing customs formalities**.
  - Self-assess duties and taxes.
  - Undergo periodic review of their systems and records rather than transaction-level checks.
- Customs authorities will rely on the trader's own data systems rather than requiring declarations — moving toward an **account-based** model.
- This represents the EU's most facilitative clearance status, designed for the most compliant operators.

**Simplified Declarations and Data Reuse:**
- The reform envisions reducing the data burden on traders by reusing commercial data (invoices, contracts, logistics records) rather than requiring separate customs declarations.
- Long-term vision: the traditional customs declaration could be replaced by **digital commercial data streams** that customs authorities access and process automatically.

### EU ICS2 (Import Control System 2)

ICS2 is the EU's pre-arrival security and safety data system, being rolled out in phases:

- **Release 1 (March 2021):** Postal and express carriers submit a minimum data set for all consignments entering the EU.
- **Release 2 (March 2023):** Airlines (general cargo) submit Entry Summary Declarations (ENS) with enhanced data.
- **Release 3 (March 2025):** Maritime, rail, and road carriers submit ENS data. Full data set requirements apply across all transport modes.

ICS2 requires **advance electronic data submission** before goods arrive in the EU, enabling risk-based screening. Non-compliance results in cargo holds and penalties.

### EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

- CBAM imposes a carbon cost on imports of carbon-intensive goods to prevent "carbon leakage" (where production moves to countries with lower carbon costs).
- **Scope:** Cement, iron and steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen. Expected to expand to additional sectors.
- **Transitional phase (October 2023 – December 2025):** Importers must report embedded carbon emissions but do not yet pay CBAM certificates.
- **Definitive phase (January 2026):** Importers must purchase CBAM certificates equivalent to the carbon price that would have been paid if goods were produced under the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). The EU's free allowances under ETS will be phased out correspondingly through 2034.
- **Implication for clearance:** CBAM adds a new data element (verified embedded emissions) and a new financial obligation (CBAM certificates) to the import process for covered goods.

### EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)

- Effective December 30, 2025 (large operators) / June 30, 2026 (SMEs).
- Prohibits placing on the EU market products associated with deforestation or forest degradation.
- **Covered products:** Cattle, cocoa, coffee, oil palm, rubber, soya, wood — and derived products (leather, chocolate, furniture, tires, etc.).
- Importers must submit a **due diligence statement** with geolocation data of production plots, proving that products were not grown on land deforested after December 31, 2020.
- Adds new data requirements to customs declarations for covered goods.

### Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

- Requires large EU companies and non-EU companies with significant EU revenue to conduct human rights and environmental due diligence across their value chains.
- While not a customs regulation per se, it creates supply chain transparency obligations that intersect with customs compliance (particularly origin and supplier verification).

## 5. United Kingdom: Post-Brexit Border Regime

### Border Target Operating Model (BTOM)

Following Brexit, the UK has been implementing its own independent border controls. The Border Target Operating Model (BTOM), published in August 2023, sets out the phased introduction of checks:

- **January 2024:** Health certificates required for medium-risk animal products, plants, and plant products from the EU.
- **April 2024:** Documentary and physical checks on medium-risk goods at border control posts.
- **October 2024:** Safety and security declarations (S&S) required for EU imports.
- **2025-2026:** Further digitization and risk-based targeting.

### UK Single Trade Window

- The UK is developing a Single Trade Window (STW) to allow traders to submit border data once, which is then shared with all relevant government departments (HMRC, DEFRA, Home Office, etc.).
- Progressive rollout began in 2024, with full capabilities expected by 2026-2027.
- The STW aims to reduce duplication and simplify the trader experience at the UK border.

### UK De Minimis Changes

- The UK currently maintains a GBP 135 de minimis threshold for customs duties (VAT applies from the first pound via the UK's own e-commerce VAT collection mechanism).
- The UK government has signaled it will **lower or eliminate the customs duty de minimis** threshold by 2029, aligning with the global trend.

### Customs Declaration Service (CDS)

- The UK's legacy customs system (CHIEF — Customs Handling of Import and Export Freight) was fully replaced by the Customs Declaration Service (CDS) in 2023.
- CDS is the digital platform for all UK customs declarations, supporting the full range of entry types and procedures.

## 6. CBP 21st Century Customs Framework (21CCF)

### A Foundational Rethinking of US Customs Law

The 21st Century Customs Framework is CBP's flagship legislative initiative to modernize the statutory foundation of US customs operations. While not yet enacted as of January 2026, it has broad bipartisan support and industry backing.

**Key Proposals:**

**Account-Based Processing:**
- Shift from **transaction-by-transaction** clearance to **account-based** processing.
- Instead of examining every individual entry, CBP would assess traders holistically — evaluating their overall compliance record, systems, and controls.
- High-trust traders (similar to C-TPAT tier 3 or EU "Trust and Check") would receive streamlined clearance with post-entry audit rather than pre-release inspection.

**Expanded Data Sources:**
- Currently, CBP can only require data from the importer of record and licensed customs broker.
- Under 21CCF, CBP would be authorized to collect data from **any party in the supply chain** — including foreign sellers, e-commerce platforms, fulfillment centers, logistics providers, and payment processors.
- This is critical for e-commerce, where the importer of record may have limited knowledge of the goods.

**De Minimis Modernization:**
- 21CCF would give CBP authority to adjust de minimis treatment based on risk, country of origin, and product category — rather than the current all-or-nothing approach.
- This could enable a tiered system where low-risk, trusted-origin goods retain facilitated treatment while high-risk categories require full entry.

**Technology Modernization:**
- Authorization for CBP to invest in next-generation technology, including AI/ML-based risk assessment, real-time data analytics, and modernized trader-facing systems.
- Potential replacement or major upgrade of the Automated Commercial Environment (ACE), which has been the US trade processing backbone since the early 2010s.

### ACE 2.0

- The Automated Commercial Environment (ACE) is the primary US system for processing trade data.
- CBP has acknowledged that ACE, while a major improvement over its predecessor (ACS), is reaching the limits of its architecture — particularly for handling e-commerce volumes and real-time data processing.
- ACE 2.0 planning envisions a cloud-based, API-first platform with enhanced automation, better trader interfaces, and integration with partner government agency systems.

## 7. Global De Minimis Trend

The elimination or reduction of de minimis thresholds is a **global phenomenon**, not limited to the US:

| Jurisdiction | Current De Minimis | Planned Change | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **United States** | $800 (suspended) | All imports require formal entry | Effective Aug 29, 2025 |
| **European Union** | EUR 150 (duty) | EUR 3/item flat duty for e-commerce | July 2026 |
| **United Kingdom** | GBP 135 (duty) | Elimination planned | By 2029 |
| **Thailand** | THB 1,500 | Eliminated | 2024 |
| **Indonesia** | $3 (already very low) | Enhanced enforcement | Ongoing |
| **India** | Effectively eliminated | Duties on all imports | Already in effect |
| **Brazil** | $50 (reinstated with conditions) | Enhanced platform liability | 2024-2025 |
| **South Africa** | ZAR 500 | Under review for reduction | Under review |

**Driving Factors:**
- Governments are losing significant customs revenue from untaxed e-commerce imports.
- Domestic manufacturers and retailers argue de minimis creates an unfair competitive advantage for foreign e-commerce sellers.
- Security and enforcement agencies cannot screen millions of unexamined parcels for fentanyl, counterfeits, and unsafe goods.
- The political environment in many countries favors protectionist measures.

**Impact on Express Carriers:**
- Every de minimis reduction or elimination creates a step-function increase in formal clearance volume.
- The cumulative global effect is that the "easy" parcel — the one that crosses borders with minimal data and no duties — is rapidly disappearing.
- Carriers must invest in clearance capacity, technology, and data quality on a global scale.

## 8. WCO Strategic Direction and WTO TFA

### World Customs Organization (WCO)

The WCO's strategic direction for the 2022-2028 period emphasizes:

- **Digital Customs:** Transition from document-based to data-centric customs, with machine-to-machine communication replacing manual filing.
- **WCO Data Model:** Standardized data set (version 3.x+) for cross-border regulatory information exchange, foundational to interoperability between customs administrations.
- **Single Window Environments:** The WCO Single Window Compendium provides implementation guidance for countries building single data submission portals.
- **Advance Electronic Information (AEI):** Mandatory pre-arrival electronic data submission becoming the global norm.
- **Authorized Economic Operator (AEO):** Expanding the SAFE Framework's AEO concept, with an emphasis on mutual recognition agreements (MRAs) between countries.
- **Digital Customs Maturity Model:** A self-assessment framework for customs administrations to evaluate and progress their digital capabilities — from paper-based to fully automated.
- **Emerging Technology:** WCO is exploring AI/ML for risk assessment, blockchain for supply chain transparency, and IoT for cargo monitoring through its annual Technology Conference.

**The WCO's North Star:** Customs that is "invisible to compliant traders" — data flows seamlessly, risk is assessed algorithmically, goods are pre-cleared, and physical intervention is the exception. Sometimes described as "customs in the cloud."

### WTO Trade Facilitation Agreement (TFA)

The WTO TFA, which entered into force in February 2017, is the most significant multilateral trade agreement in two decades. It commits WTO members to:

- **Publication and availability** of customs laws, regulations, and procedures.
- **Advance rulings** on tariff classification, origin, and valuation.
- **Pre-arrival processing** of import documentation.
- **Electronic payment** of duties and taxes.
- **Risk management** in customs procedures.
- **Post-clearance audit** as an alternative to pre-release inspection.
- **Single Window** implementation.
- **Authorized Operator** programs.
- **Border agency cooperation** (both domestic and cross-border).

**Implementation Status (as of late 2025):**
- Overall implementation rate: approximately **80%** of all TFA provisions globally.
- Developed countries have largely implemented all provisions.
- Developing countries continue to implement under "Category B" (with transition periods) and "Category C" (requiring technical assistance).
- The WTO estimates that full TFA implementation could reduce trade costs by **14.3%** for developing countries and **10.4%** for developed countries.

## 9. Partner Government Agency (PGA) Requirements

### The Multi-Agency Border

Customs clearance is not solely a customs authority function. In the United States alone, **49 federal agencies** have authority over goods at the border. Key PGA requirements include:

**Food and Drug Administration (FDA):**
- Prior Notice of imported food (must be submitted 2-24 hours before arrival depending on transport mode).
- Registration of food facilities under the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA).
- Foreign Supplier Verification Program (FSVP) — importers must verify that foreign suppliers' food safety practices meet US standards.
- Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA) — full traceability requirements for pharmaceutical products (phased implementation through 2025-2026).
- Medical device registration and listing.

**USDA / APHIS:**
- Phytosanitary certificates for plant and plant products.
- Veterinary certificates for animal products.
- Lacey Act declarations for wood and plant-based products.
- COOL (Country of Origin Labeling) for certain agricultural products.

**Environmental Protection Agency (EPA):**
- TSCA (Toxic Substances Control Act) import certification for chemicals.
- Vehicle and engine emissions compliance.
- Pesticide import requirements under FIFRA.

**Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC):**
- Certificate of Compliance for consumer products, particularly children's products.
- CPSA (Consumer Product Safety Act) import surveillance.
- Risk Assessment Methodology (RAM) pilot program for e-commerce shipments.

**Federal Communications Commission (FCC):**
- Equipment authorization for electronic devices that emit radio frequency energy.

**Other Notable PGA Requirements:**
- **TTB (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau):** Permits and certificates for alcohol, tobacco, and firearms.
- **DEA (Drug Enforcement Administration):** Import permits for controlled substances and precursor chemicals.
- **Fish and Wildlife Service:** CITES permits, Lacey Act declarations for wildlife and wildlife products.
- **NHTSA (National Highway Traffic Safety Administration):** Motor vehicle safety compliance.

**INFORM Consumers Act (effective June 2023):**
- Requires online marketplaces to collect, verify, and disclose information about high-volume third-party sellers.
- While not a customs regulation per se, it creates data collection obligations for e-commerce platforms that intersect with customs compliance (seller identification, product origin).

### PGA Coordination Challenges

- Each PGA has its own data requirements, filing timelines, and systems.
- While ACE serves as the "single window" for US trade processing, not all PGAs are fully integrated into ACE.
- PGA holds account for a significant percentage of clearance delays — goods may clear customs but be held awaiting PGA review.
- E-commerce compounds the problem: consumer products may trigger multiple PGA requirements (FDA for a cosmetic, CPSC for a toy, FCC for an electronic device) — each requiring separate data and potentially separate review.

## 10. Regulatory Velocity: The Compounding Challenge

Perhaps the most significant meta-trend is the **accelerating pace of regulatory change**. Consider the compliance requirements that have been introduced, expanded, or fundamentally altered in the 2023-2026 period alone:

- US de minimis elimination
- IEEPA reciprocal tariffs (multiple rounds of changes)
- Section 301 tariff increases (post-four-year review)
- UFLPA enforcement expansion to downstream products
- EU Customs Reform Package
- EU CBAM transitional and definitive phases
- EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)
- EU ICS2 Releases 2 and 3
- UK BTOM phased implementation
- UK Single Trade Window rollout
- Thailand de minimis elimination
- Brazil e-commerce platform rules
- CBP 21CCF legislative progress
- DSCSA pharmaceutical traceability (final phase)
- Multiple rounds of Section 232 modifications

This velocity of change makes it nearly impossible for compliance teams staffed at historical levels to keep pace. Regulatory change management — monitoring, interpreting, implementing, and training on new requirements — has become a core operational discipline, not an occasional project.

## Key Implications for FedEx

1. **Volume Shock:** De minimis elimination globally will continue to drive exponential growth in formal entry volumes, requiring massive scalability in clearance operations and technology.

2. **Data is the New Bottleneck:** Every regulatory change adds new data requirements. The ability to capture, validate, and transmit complete, accurate trade data at the point of origin is becoming the single most critical capability in clearance.

3. **Duty Calculation is a Moving Target:** Stacked tariff programs, frequent rate changes, and complex rules of origin make accurate duty calculation exponentially harder. Errors carry increasing financial and legal risk.

4. **Compliance Scope is Expanding:** Clearance is no longer just about duties and classifications. UFLPA, CBAM, EUDR, DSCSA, and other programs require entirely new categories of data (supply chain traceability, carbon emissions, deforestation-free sourcing) that are outside traditional customs expertise.

5. **Regulatory Divergence is Growing:** While organizations like WCO promote harmonization, the reality is that major jurisdictions (US, EU, UK) are increasingly pursuing independent and sometimes conflicting regulatory approaches — requiring country-specific compliance capabilities.

6. **Technology is Becoming Mandatory:** The volume, complexity, and velocity of regulatory change exceed what manual processes and legacy systems can handle. AI-assisted classification, automated filing, and real-time regulatory monitoring are becoming operational necessities, not competitive advantages.
